# Rajasthan in 7 Days — Forts, Palaces & Desert Sunsets (Complete Guide)

**Trip Type:** Heritage + Culture + Desert Adventure
**Duration:** 7 Days / 6 Nights
**Budget:** ₹18,000–₹55,000 per person
**Best Time:** October–February
**Difficulty:** Easy

---

## Why Rajasthan Never Gets Old

I've done Rajasthan three times. Each time I find something new. That's the thing about a state with 6 UNESCO sites, 300+ forts and palaces, and a culture so vivid it feels like walking into a painting.

This 7-day route covers the classic circuit — Jaipur → Jodhpur → Jaisalmer → (optional: Udaipur) — with enough depth to feel like a real traveller, not a checklist tourist.

## Day 1 — Jaipur: The Pink City's Greatest Hits

**Arrive morning.** Jaipur works perfectly as a base for the first two days.

**City Palace (₹500 entry):** The Maharaja still lives in part of this palace. The courtyards, textile museum, and armoury are world-class. Allow 2 hours minimum.

**Jantar Mantar (₹200):** Built in 1734, this astronomical observatory uses giant stone instruments to track planetary positions with stunning accuracy. The Samrat Yantra (world's largest sundial, accurate to 2 seconds) is mind-bending.

**Hawa Mahal (₹50):** The "Palace of Winds" — 953 small windows built so royal ladies could watch street processions without being seen. Best photographed from the tea shop across the street at golden hour.

**Dinner:** MI Road for chaat, then Niro's Restaurant (since 1949) for proper Rajasthani thali — dal baati churma, ker sangri, gatte ki sabzi. ₹400–₹600/head.

**Where I stayed:** Dera Rawatsar Haveli, Jaipur — ₹2,800/night (heritage property, rooftop breakfast)

---

## Day 2 — Amber Fort & Nahargarh

**Amber Fort (₹550):** Best fort in India, full stop. Perched on a hillside 11km from Jaipur, built in 1592. The Sheesh Mahal (Mirror Palace) inside has a ceiling entirely encrusted with mirror mosaics — one candle lights the whole room. Take the shuttle jeep up (₹100) rather than elephant — more ethical, faster.

**Jaigarh Fort (linked with Amber, ₹100 extra):** Contains the world's largest wheeled cannon. The views over the Aravalli hills are extraordinary.

**Evening:** Nahargarh Fort. Sunset from the terrace here is peak Jaipur — the pink city spreads below you, the setting sun turns everything amber, and 15 other travellers from around the world are all taking the same photo. Worth it every time. Stay for dinner at Padao (rooftop restaurant inside the fort, ₹600–₹900).

---

## Day 3 — Jodhpur: The Blue City

**Overnight train from Jaipur:** Mandore Express or Ala Hazrat Express (5–6 hours, sleeper ₹200, 3AC ₹500). Arrive morning.

**Mehrangarh Fort (₹600):** The most dramatic fort in Rajasthan. Rises 125m above the city on a sheer rock face. Inside: eight palaces, a stunning museum, and views of the blue city below. The blue houses were originally painted by Brahmins to denote caste; the tradition caught on and the whole city turned blue. The audio guide (₹100) is excellent.

**The Blue City lanes:** Walk down from the fort into the tangle of indigo alleys below. Nai Sarak and Navchokiya areas — tiny shops selling spices, puppets, block-print fabric. Get lost on purpose. You'll find better shops than anything at the main market.

**Jaswant Thada (₹50):** A marble cenotaph gleaming white against the blue sky. Quiet, beautiful, almost always uncrowded.

**Dinner at Indique** (rooftop, ₹700/head): Laal maas (fiery mutton curry), safed maas (cream-based), makhaniya lassi. The view of Mehrangarh lit up at night is spectacular.

---

## Day 4 — Drive to Jaisalmer: Through the Desert

Hire a taxi or take the Rajasthan State Bus. The drive (4.5 hours) crosses the scrub desert of Thar — sparse, gorgeous in early morning light.

**Stop at Osian (75km from Jodhpur):** Cluster of 8th–11th century Hindu and Jain temples, almost unknown to international tourists. The Sachiya Mata temple on a hilltop and the carved Mahavira Temple below are extraordinary. No entry fee. An hour here is one of the most memorable things I've done in Rajasthan.

**Arrive Jaisalmer afternoon.** Check into a heritage haveli inside or just outside the fort walls.

---

## Day 5 — Jaisalmer: The Golden City

**Jaisalmer Fort:** Unlike most Indian forts, people live inside this one. It's a living fort — 4,000 residents, shops, temples, restaurants within the ramparts. The fort glows golden at sunrise and sunset (Jaisalmer stone is yellow limestone). Walk the ramparts, visit the Jain temples inside (7th century, free with a small donation), eat at a rooftop café overlooking the desert.

**Patwon Ki Haveli (₹100):** Five ornately carved mansions built by a family of brocade merchants in the 19th century. The jaali (lattice) stonework is impossibly intricate.

**Gadisar Lake (free):** An artificial lake built in 1367. At dawn, migratory birds cover the water. The carved chhatris (pavilions) at the gateway make for beautiful photos.

**Bazaar evening:** Sadar Market for silver jewellery, block print scarves, camel leather goods. Always bargain. Starting price is usually 3x fair price.

---

## Day 6 — Sam Sand Dunes: The Desert Night

This is what most people come to Jaisalmer for.

**Sam Sand Dunes (42km from Jaisalmer):** The Thar Desert in its classic form — undulating orange dunes, no trees, silence so complete you can hear your own heartbeat.

**Options:**
- **Budget:** Shared jeep from Jaisalmer (₹200) + government camel ride (₹500)
- **Better:** Hire private jeep (₹1,200 round trip) + overnight tent camp

**The overnight desert camp** is the real experience. A good camp (Suryagarh, Desert Haveli, or smaller family camps) includes: jeep transfer, camel ride to the dunes, sunset, Rajasthani folk music around a bonfire, dinner, tent accommodation. ₹3,000–₹8,000/person depending on camp quality.

The silence at 2am in the desert, lying on a dune looking at more stars than you thought existed — this is why people come to Rajasthan.

---

## Day 7 — Sunrise Dunes, Departure

Wake at 5:30am. Walk up the nearest dune before the day-trippers arrive (they start at 7am). The desert at dawn is pink and copper and completely still. Spend an hour. Then: breakfast, check-out, departure.

Train or flight back from Jaisalmer (direct flights to Delhi, Mumbai — book in advance, they fill fast).

## Don't Miss, Don't Bother

**Don't miss:**
- Mehrangarh Fort museum (far better than most state museums)
- Osian temples (almost no tourists)
- Overnight desert camp (the real reason to come to Jaisalmer)
- Rajasthani thali (dal baati churma is the one — rich, heavy, extraordinary)

**Don't bother:**
- Camel rides on main Jaisalmer roads (overpriced tourist trap)
- Bikaner (unless you have extra days — good fort but out of the main circuit)
- Puskar if you're not there for the November camel fair
