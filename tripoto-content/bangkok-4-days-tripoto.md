# Bangkok in 4 Days — Street Food, Grand Palaces & Rooftop Bars

**Trip Type:** Culture + Food + City
**Duration:** 4 Days / 3 Nights
**Budget:** $25–$100/day per person (₹2,000–₹8,300)
**Best Time:** November–February (cool season)
**Difficulty:** Easy

---

## Bangkok: The City That Never Lets You Be Bored

Bangkok moves fast. The street food stalls open at 5am. The temples fill with worshippers at dawn. The Chao Phraya river carries goods from dawn to midnight. The night markets run until 2am.

This city has been the most-visited in the world multiple times. That's not marketing — it's because Bangkok delivers on every promise it makes.

---

## Day 1 — Grand Palace & the River

**Grand Palace (650 THB, ~₹1,550):** The most important site in Thailand. The entire palace complex — home to Thai kings for 150 years, now ceremonial — is extraordinary. The Emerald Buddha (actually jadeite, 66cm tall) inside Wat Phra Kaew is considered the most sacred image in Thailand. Dress code strictly enforced: no shorts, no sleeveless tops, no sandals. They loan sarongs at the gate. Allow 3 hours.

**Wat Pho (200 THB):** The Temple of the Reclining Buddha — a 46m gilded image, 15m tall, filling an entire building. Also contains Thailand's first university (traditional medicine, massage). Genuinely one of the most impressive religious sculptures in Asia. The massage school here offers 30-minute traditional Thai massage for 260 THB — possibly the best-value thing you can do in Bangkok.

**Chao Phraya River Express (16–32 THB):** Take the public boat (orange flag express) up and down the river. This is Bangkok's original highway — and watching the city from the water, with temples and markets and longtail boats and cargo barges, is extraordinary.

**Khao San Road (evening):** Yes, it's touristy. Yes, the bucket cocktails are terrible. Yes, it's also alive with backpackers from 40 countries, pad thai stalls at 11pm, and a general chaos that's entertaining for one evening. Then move on.

**Where I stayed:** Riva Arun Bangkok (₹4,500/night, views of Wat Arun from the rooftop). Budget: NapPark Hostel, Phra Arthit Road — ₹800/night.

## Day 2 — Chatuchak & Street Food Deep-Dive

**Chatuchak Weekend Market (Saturday–Sunday, free entry):** The largest market in Asia. 15,000 stalls across 27 acres. Clothes, antiques, plants, art, street food, live animals, vintage items, hand-made crafts. It takes a full morning to cover even 20% of it. Go before 11am — it gets extremely hot and crowded by noon. The street food inside Chatuchak is excellent — pork satay, mango sticky rice, fresh coconut ice cream.

**If not a weekend:** Platinum Fashion Mall and Pratunam Market fill the gap. Less character but huge variety of clothing.

**Afternoon: Or Tor Kor Market (500m from Chatuchak, free):** Bangkok's best fresh food market. No tourist food here — this is where restaurant chefs buy. Extraordinary tropical fruits (rambutans, longans, mangosteen, rose apples), prepared foods, dried goods. The durian section alone is worth the visit. Sample freely.

**Street Food Tour of Yaowarat (Chinatown):** Bangkok's Chinatown is best at night (from 6pm). Yaowarat Road fills with steaming carts — roast duck, char siu pork, crab omelets, oyster cakes, boat noodles. This is where serious Bangkok foodies go. Don't plan dinner anywhere else; just walk and eat. Budget: 300–600 THB for a complete food tour by walking.

## Day 3 — Temples + Floating Market + Rooftop

**Amphawa Floating Market (80km from Bangkok, best Friday–Sunday):** A canal-side market where vendors sell food from their boats. More authentic than the closer Damnoen Saduak (which is extremely touristy). The journey by mini-bus (130 THB from Mo Chit station) and the canal boat rides back through the canals in the evening are part of the experience. Firefly watching tours (100 THB) happen after dark — paddling through dark canals with trees of blinking fireflies above.

**Alternatively (if staying in Bangkok):**

**Wat Arun (100 THB):** The Temple of Dawn, on the west bank of the Chao Phraya. Covered in porcelain fragments, extraordinary at close range. Best photographed from the opposite bank at sunrise.

**Jim Thompson House (200 THB):** The home of the American silk trader who "disappeared" in Malaysia in 1967. A collection of six traditional Thai houses filled with Asian antiques. The mystery of Thompson's disappearance — never solved — adds atmosphere.

**Sky Bar, Lebua State Tower (free to enter, drinks from 400 THB):** The most famous rooftop bar in Bangkok. Where The Hangover Part II was filmed. 64 floors above the city. Dress code. Overpriced drinks. View so good you don't care.

**Alternative rooftop:** Octave Rooftop Bar, Marriott Sukhumvit — three-level rooftop, 300 THB cocktails, 360° city views, no dress code.

## Day 4 — Ayutthaya Day Trip & Departure

**Ayutthaya** (80km north of Bangkok, 1.5 hours by train from Hua Lamphong station, 15–20 THB): The ancient capital of the Kingdom of Siam, destroyed by the Burmese in 1767. Now a UNESCO World Heritage Site — hundreds of temple ruins, headless Buddha statues, restored chedi.

**Must-see:**
- Wat Mahathat: The famous Buddha head entwined in banyan tree roots (60 THB entry)
- Wat Phra Si Sanphet: Three giant chedis in a row — the classic Ayutthaya image
- Wat Chaiwatthanaram: Restored temple on the river bank, stunning at sunset

**Rent a bicycle in Ayutthaya (60–80 THB/day):** The ruins are spread across a large area. Cycling between them through the quiet streets, past lotus ponds and local school children, is the right way to do it.

**Return to Bangkok by late afternoon.** Airport by evening for night flights.

## Bangkok Tips That Matter

- **BTS Skytrain is your best friend.** 16–59 THB per trip, air-conditioned, no traffic. Get a Rabbit Card (₹300 deposit, reload as needed) for convenience.
- **Grab > taxis.** Taxis are supposed to use meters but often refuse to on busy routes. Grab shows the price upfront.
- **7-Eleven is genuinely useful.** Thailand's 7-Elevens stock excellent food: fresh pastries, onigiri, sandwiches, coffee. A 7-Eleven breakfast costs 50 THB.
- **Always negotiate at markets except supermarkets.** Opening price is typically 2–3x expected sale price.
- **Dress for temples.** Carry a thin scarf to cover shoulders — it doubles as sun protection and temple-entry pass.
