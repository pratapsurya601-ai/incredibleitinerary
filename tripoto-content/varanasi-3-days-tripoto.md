# Varanasi in 3 Days — The City That Teaches You to Live

**Trip Type:** Spiritual + Cultural
**Duration:** 3 Days / 2 Nights
**Budget:** ₹4,000–₹15,000 per person (excluding flights)
**Best Time:** October–March
**Difficulty:** Easy (mentally and emotionally challenging)

---

## What Varanasi Really Is

Varanasi is the oldest continuously inhabited city on earth. It has been here for 3,500 years. People come here to die because Hindus believe that dying in Varanasi brings moksha — liberation from the cycle of rebirth.

Every day, dozens of bodies are cremated on the ghats alongside the Ganges. Pilgrims bathe in the same water. Children play cricket. Priests chant. Tourists stare. Buffalo wade. The laundry is done. Dogs sleep in the sun.

It's the most alive place I've ever been to.

Nothing prepares you for it. Nothing should.

## Day 1 — Dawn on the Ganges: The Most Powerful Morning of Your Life

**Wake up at 5am.** This is not optional.

Walk to any ghat (or hire an auto for ₹100) and hire a boat. The standard rate is ₹200–₹300/person for an hour-long rowing boat at sunrise. Negotiate, but don't be too aggressive — these boatmen get up at 3am to prepare.

**What you'll see:** As the sun rises over the opposite bank (the east bank, Varanasi always faces east), the ghats come alive simultaneously. Thousands of Hindus in the water performing their morning ablutions. Priests beginning their rituals. Burning ghats with the first cremations of the day. Temple bells ringing. Flowers floating on the water. The Ganges itself turning from grey to orange to gold.

I didn't take a single photo that morning. I just watched.

**Dasaswamedh Ghat:** The main ghat, always active. Priests perform elaborate Ganga Aarti here every evening (and a smaller dawn version).

**Manikarnika Ghat:** The burning ghat. 24 hours a day, every day, bodies are cremated here. Photography is strictly prohibited. You can watch from a distance or from a boat. It is confronting. It is also, somehow, beautiful — the way Varanasi has been doing this for 3,000 years as the world changed around it.

**Breakfast:** Kachori sabzi at a street stall near the ghats. ₹40 for a generous plate. Eat with your hands like everyone else.

**Where I stayed:** Brijrama Palace — ₹6,000/night (a Maharaja's palace converted to a heritage hotel, directly on the Ganges). Budget option: Stops Hostel or Moustache Varanasi — ₹600/night (dorm).

## Day 1 Afternoon — The Lanes of the Old City

Varanasi's old city is a maze of lanes called gallis — some as narrow as 60cm — that connect the ghats to the interior of the city. You will get lost. That's the point.

**Vishwanath Lane (Vishwanath Galli):** Leads to the Golden Temple (Kashi Vishwanath). Non-Hindus cannot enter the main sanctum but can see the gold-plated spire. The lane itself is extraordinary — silk shops, flower sellers, sweet shops, sadhus, pilgrims from every corner of India.

**Kashi Vishwanath Temple (recently renovated):** One of the 12 Jyotirlingas (sacred Shiva shrines). Entry now much easier after the 2021 corridor redevelopment. Shoes outside, phones checked in. The energy inside is unlike anything else in India.

**Kedar Ghat area:** One of the quieter ghats. Sit on the steps and watch boats pass for an hour. A chai seller will find you.

**Evening:** Dashashwamedh Ghat Aarti. 7pm every day (sunset time). Seven priests perform a synchronized fire ritual with huge lamps, incense, and conch shells. Thousands watch from the ghats and from boats. Arrive 45 minutes early for a seat. This is one of the top 5 things to witness in India.

## Day 2 — Sarnath: Where Buddhism Was Born

**Sarnath** (10km from Varanasi, auto ₹200 round trip):

After attaining enlightenment in Bodh Gaya, the Buddha came here to deliver his first sermon in 528 BCE. The site of that sermon — the Deer Park — is now one of the most sacred places in Buddhism.

**Dhamek Stupa:** A 43m cylindrical stupa built in 500 CE. The carved geometric and floral patterns around the base are some of the finest stone carving in India. Entry ₹40.

**Sarnath Museum:** A 1-km walk from the stupa. Contains the Lion Capital of Ashoka — the 3rd century BCE sculpture that became India's national emblem. You've seen this on every coin and note; seeing the original is genuinely moving. ₹5 entry (yes, ₹5).

**Tibetan Monastery:** Near the main complex. Monks in robes, spinning prayer wheels, Tibetan thangkas on the walls. Free entry.

**Mulagandha Kuti Vihar:** A modern Mahabodhi temple with frescoes depicting scenes from the Buddha's life painted by a Japanese artist in the 1930s. Serene, beautiful, almost always quiet. Free.

## Day 2 Evening — Boats and Bhang

**Evening boat ride:** Second boat ride, this time at sunset. Different light, different mood. The ghats in the orange evening light have been painted by every great Indian artist.

**Bhang lassi:** Legal in Varanasi. Available at government-licensed bhang shops near the ghats. Bhang is cannabis-infused milk — served as a lassi. Strong. Very strong. Don't drink more than one. Don't make plans for the evening.

---

## Day 3 — Deeper Varanasi

**Tulsi Manas Temple:** Built in 1964 in white marble, on the site where Tulsidas wrote the Ramcharitmanas (Hindi Ramayana) in the 16th century. Scenes from the Ramayana depicted in marble throughout. Free entry.

**Ramnagar Fort (across the river, free boat crossing ₹10):** The palace of the Maharaja of Varanasi. His family still lives here. The museum inside has palanquins, vintage cars, astronomical equipment, and embroidered elephants. ₹30 entry.

**Silk shopping:** Varanasi is the silk capital of India. Banarasi silk sarees are woven here — pure silk, with gold and silver thread work. A genuine Banarasi saree starts at ₹2,500 and goes to ₹50,000+. The government handloom emporium (honest prices) is on Varun Nagar road. The weavers' neighborhood of Madanpura lets you watch the looms work — incredible.

**Final ghat sit:** Spend your last evening simply sitting on any quiet ghat. Assi Ghat has a good mix of locals, students, pilgrims, and travellers. Students from BHU (Banaras Hindu University) play music here in the evenings.

## What Varanasi Will Do to You

People come here expecting a spiritual experience and get a culture shock. People come expecting chaos and find unexpected peace. People come for 3 days and stay for 3 weeks.

Varanasi doesn't conform to expectations. That's why it's still the same city it was 3,000 years ago while everything around it changed.

Go with no plans. Get lost in the lanes. Take the dawn boat. Watch the Aarti. Let it affect you.
