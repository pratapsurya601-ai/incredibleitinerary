## Tokyo: The City That Makes Every Other City Seem Slightly Disorganised

Tokyo runs on time. The trains are never late (the average delay per year is under 1 minute). The streets are spotless. The convenience stores sell better food than most restaurants elsewhere in the world.

But underneath the extraordinary efficiency, Tokyo is also one of the most interesting cultural cities on earth. 700-year-old temples in the middle of a skyscraper district. Geisha walking to work in Yanaka. Tsukiji fishmongers cutting tuna at 4am.

Five days is enough to love Tokyo. It takes a lifetime to know it.

## Day 1 — Shinjuku & Arrival

**Fly into Narita or Haneda.** Get the Narita Express (NEX) to Shinjuku (3,070 JPY, 90 minutes) or Haneda's monorail to Hamamatsucho (550 JPY, 25 minutes). Buy a Suica card at the airport machine — it works on all trains, buses, and in 7-Elevens. Load ¥5,000–¥10,000.

**Shinjuku at night:** Your first Tokyo evening should be here. Kabukicho (Tokyo's entertainment district) lights up — pachinko parlours, izakayas, ramen stalls, karaoke bars. Golden Gai: 200 tiny bars packed into a block, each serving 5–8 people. One drink per bar is the culture. The atmosphere is 1960s Tokyo preserved in amber.

**Dinner:** Ichiran Ramen, Shinjuku — individual booths where you eat alone without interacting with staff. Ordering via form. Broth so precise it's almost engineered. ¥1,000.

**Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building (free):** 45th floor observation deck, free, open till 11pm. The view over Tokyo at night — an unbroken grid of lights extending to every horizon — is one of the great city views.

**Where I stayed:** Park Hyatt Tokyo, Shinjuku (yes, the Lost in Translation hotel) — ¥70,000/night. Budget: Khaosan Tokyo Ninja — ¥3,500/night (excellent hostel, great location).

## Day 2 — Asakusa & Ueno: Old Tokyo

**Sensō-ji Temple (free, always open):** Tokyo's oldest temple, built in 628 CE. The approach (Nakamise-dori shopping street) leads to the Kaminarimon Gate — the most photographed gate in Japan. Inside: incense smoke, monks, school trips, and a genuine sense of the sacred despite 30 million visitors a year. Go before 7am for the quiet version.

**Asakusa streets:** The area around Sensō-ji is Tokyo's most traditional neighbourhood. Rickshaw rides, kimono rental, craft shops, ningyo-yaki (bird-shaped cakes). Buy ningyo-yaki warm from the stall for ¥200.

**Ueno Park and Museums:** The park contains no fewer than 9 museums and a zoo. The Tokyo National Museum (¥1,000) has the finest collection of Japanese art in the world — 110,000 objects including samurai armour, Edo woodblock prints, and Buddhist sculpture. Allow 3 hours.

**Ameyoko Market (free):** A covered market street from Ueno to Okachimachi, originally a black market after WWII, now a bustling street market for food, cosmetics, clothing, and imported goods. Best in the afternoon.

## Day 3 — Shibuya, Harajuku & Omotesando

**Shibuya Crossing:** Go at 8am (morning rush) or 6pm (evening rush). The world's busiest pedestrian crossing — up to 3,000 people cross simultaneously every 2 minutes. Watch from the Starbucks on the corner (arrive early for a window seat) or from Mag's Park or Scramble Square observation deck (¥2,000) above.

**Harajuku:** Sunday on Takeshita-dori — the narrow lane of extreme fashion, crepe stalls (¥600 for a crepe the size of your arm), thrift shops, and teenagers dressed with extraordinary creativity. Even on weekdays: good second-hand shops on the backstreets.

**Meiji Jingu Shrine (free):** From the chaos of Harajuku, a 5-minute walk to the most serene Shinto shrine in Tokyo. A 700m forested path leads to the shrine — completely silent, completely unlike the city around it. If you're there on a weekend morning, you'll likely see a traditional Shinto wedding.

**Omotesando (Tokyo's Champs-Élysées):** From Harajuku to Aoyama — Tokyo's fashion district. The architecture is extraordinary (each major fashion house commissioned a different architect). Window shopping even if not buying.

**TeamLab Borderless or Planets (¥3,200):** Digital art installation where projections react to your movement. Immersive, beautiful, and exactly what it looks like in the photos. Book online weeks ahead.

**Dinner: Izakaya in Shibuya or Ebisu.** An izakaya is a Japanese pub with food — you order small plates while drinking. Budget ¥3,000–¥5,000/person for an excellent meal with drinks.

## Day 4 — Day Trip: Nikko or Kamakura

**Option A — Nikko (2 hours by Tobu Limited Express, ¥2,720):**
The most ornate shrine complex in Japan, set in cedar forests in the Nikko mountains. Tōshō-gū Shrine (¥1,300) — over-the-top decorative style, a total contrast to Japan's usual architectural minimalism. The three wise monkeys carving (hear no evil, see no evil, speak no evil) is one of the originals here. In autumn (October–November), the maple foliage around Nikko is extraordinary.

**Option B — Kamakura (1 hour, ¥920 from Shinjuku):**
A coastal town of temples and the Great Buddha (Kotoku-in Daibutsu — ¥300). A 13.35m bronze sitting Buddha, cast in 1252. The image is haunting and massive. The town has 19 Buddhist temples and 5 Shinto shrines within walking distance of each other. Hike between temples on the Daibutsu Hiking Course (4km through forested hills) and arrive at Hase-dera Temple (¥400) for ocean views.

---

## Day 5 — Yanaka, Akihabara & Departure

**Yanaka:** Tokyo's most preserved Edo-period neighbourhood. Small wooden houses, a cemetery (beautifully kept, locals walk through it casually), a shotengai (covered shopping street) with traditional food shops — sembei (rice crackers), dried fish, pickles. No tourists. Highly photogenic.

**Akihabara (Electric Town):** Tokyo's electronics and anime district. Multi-story electronics stores (Yodobashi Camera, BIC Camera — electronics significantly cheaper than airport duty-free). Anime figurines, manga, vintage arcade games, maid cafés. Even if you have no interest in electronics or anime, the density and visual overload of it is fascinating.

**Tsukiji Outer Market (the morning before any departure):** The inner market moved to Toyosu in 2018 but the outer market remains — fresh fish, tamagoyaki (sweet rolled omelette), excellent breakfast. Sushi for breakfast at Sushi Dai or Daiwa Sushi: ¥4,000–¥6,000 for 10-piece set. One of Tokyo's most authentic food experiences.

## Tokyo Practical Tips

- **IC Card (Suica/Pasmo):** Load it and use it everywhere. Faster than buying tickets, works on all transit.
- **Google Maps works perfectly for Tokyo transit.** It tells you exactly which car to board to be closest to the exit. Use it.
- **Convenience stores (7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson):** Genuinely excellent food — onigiri, sandwiches, hot noodles, fresh salads. A full breakfast for ¥500.
- **Cash is king:** Japan remains heavily cash-based. Get Yen from a 7-Eleven ATM (accepts foreign cards) at the airport.
- **Restaurants without English menus:** Point to a neighbouring table and say "Kore, onegaishimasu" ("This, please"). Works every time.
