## The Thing About Kerala

Kerala doesn't shout at you. It doesn't bombard you with monuments or famous landmarks. It just wraps you in green, feeds you coconut fish curry, and quietly becomes your favourite place in India.

I've travelled through 18 Indian states. Kerala is the one I think about most.

This 5-day route goes from Kochi to Munnar to Alleppey — three entirely different worlds within 250km of each other.

## Day 1 — Fort Kochi: The City That Doesn't Feel Like India

Fort Kochi is technically a city but it feels like a Mediterranean village. Portuguese churches from 1503, Dutch palaces from 1663, a Jewish synagogue from 1568, Chinese fishing nets that are 600 years old — all within walking distance of each other.

**Chinese Fishing Nets (free):** The first thing you see arriving in Fort Kochi. Enormous cantilever nets operated by teams of men using counterbalance stones. Come at sunrise or sunset — the light on the water and the silhouette of the nets is extraordinary. Fishermen will sell you their morning catch to be grilled at nearby stalls (₹200–₹400 per fish, they grill it on the spot).

**Mattancherry Palace (₹5 entry):** Built by the Portuguese in 1555, gifted to the Cochin royal family. The murals inside depicting scenes from the Ramayana and Mahabharata are among the finest in India. Almost no tourists.

**Jewish Synagogue (₹5):** The oldest active synagogue in the Commonwealth. Built in 1568. Floor tiled with hand-painted Chinese willow-pattern tiles, each unique. Closed Saturday.

**Lunch at Kashi Art Café:** The most famous café in Fort Kochi. Garden setting, excellent filter coffee, Kerala-style breakfasts. ₹200 for a full breakfast.

**Evening Kathakali show:** Fort Kochi has several venues. Kerala Kathakali Centre (₹250) does a good 45-minute excerpt with makeup demonstration beforehand. The traditional performance uses only facial muscles, eyes, and hands — a language of gesture developed over 500 years.

**Where I stayed:** Pepper Route Heritage Homestay, Fort Kochi — ₹2,200/night (heritage house, excellent breakfast)

## Day 2 — Kochi to Munnar: The Most Beautiful Drive in South India

The road from Kochi to Munnar (130km, 4 hours) climbs from sea level to 1,600m through 22 hairpin bends, waterfalls, rubber plantations, and tea estates. Hire a taxi (₹2,500) rather than taking a bus — you'll want to stop constantly.

**Cheeyappara Waterfalls** (on the Munnar road): Seven-tiered waterfall visible from the road. Slippery rocks, worth the short hike down.

**Arrive Munnar by afternoon.** Munnar sits in a bowl of tea-covered hills — every surface is a precise shade of green, trimmed by women tea-pickers who work with incredible speed.

**Evening:** Walk through the tea estates above town as the mist rolls in. It's ethereal. A Malayalam film is being made somewhere in those hills at any given time — the light is that good.

**Where I stayed:** Winds Estate Munnar — ₹3,500/night (private cottage in tea estate, surreal views)

## Day 3 — Munnar: Tea, Wildlife & Spice

**Eravikulam National Park (₹125 + ₹15 bus):** Home to the Nilgiri Tahr — a mountain goat found only in the Western Ghats. Open January–June (closed during calving season July–September). The drive up to Rajamala meadows is spectacular; the tahrs are remarkably unafraid of humans.

**Top Station (32km from Munnar):** The highest point in the Munnar region. On clear days you see into Tamil Nadu. The drive through continuous tea estates is the point — don't rush it.

**Spice Garden visit:** Several family-run spice farms offer 1-hour guided walks through their cardamom, pepper, nutmeg, vanilla, and coffee plants. ₹150/person, includes a cup of fresh cardamom tea. Buy spices directly from the farm — significantly cheaper and better quality than tourist shops. Cardamom sells for ₹800–₹1,200/100g at shops; farms sell for ₹400–₹600.

**Dinner:** A Sadhya (traditional Kerala feast on banana leaf) at any local restaurant. 20+ dishes served in a specific order. ₹200–₹350. The parippu (lentil) + ghee opening sequence is mandatory.

## Day 4 — Alleppey: The Houseboat Day

Drive from Munnar to Alleppey (3.5 hours, ₹2,800 by taxi). You're descending back through the Western Ghats to Kerala's coast and backwater network.

**The Alleppey Backwaters:** 900km of canals, lagoons, and lakes connecting villages along Kerala's coastline. People live here — cooking on their porches, pulling fish nets, taking children to school by boat. A houseboat doesn't feel like tourism; it feels like a very privileged window into how Kerala actually lives.

**Houseboat types:**
- **Budget:** Shared houseboat, dormitory style (₹2,500/person, meals included)
- **Mid-range:** Private AC houseboat, 1 bedroom (₹7,000–₹12,000/boat, meals included)
- **Luxury:** Premium houseboat, sundeck + chef (₹18,000–₹35,000/boat)

**My recommendation:** Go mid-range. The extra ₹4,000 over budget gets you a private boat, your own cook, and the ability to stop wherever you want. On a shared boat you're on someone else's schedule.

**What a day looks like:** Slow morning on the water watching egrets fish in the rice paddies. Lunch on the deck (coconut rice, fish moilee, thoran — vegetables cooked with grated coconut). Stop at a village for tender coconut (₹20 from the tree). Sunset on the deck with a Kerala toddy (coconut palm wine, ₹60/glass). Dinner as the fireflies appear. Sleep to the sound of water.

No Instagram photo does it justice.

---

## Day 5 — Marari Beach & Departure

Morning: slow breakfast on the houseboat before disembarking.

**Marari Beach (25km from Alleppey):** One of Kerala's finest beaches — long, clean, lined with fishing villages and coconut palms. Completely different from Goa's touristy beaches. Fishing boats launch at dawn; women dry fish on the beach. It's working, real, beautiful.

Drive back to Kochi Airport (1.5 hours from Alleppey). Flights from here connect to all major Indian cities and many international destinations.

## What Surprised Me Most About Kerala

- **How clean it is.** Kerala consistently ranks as India's cleanest state. It's visible everywhere.
- **The literacy.** 96.2% literacy rate — the highest in India. People are well-read, opinionated, curious.
- **The food.** Every meal was exceptional. Not just "good for India" good — genuinely world-class.
- **How little tourism has ruined it.** Despite being India's most-visited state, the core culture feels intact.

---

## 3 Things Most Tourists Get Wrong

1. **Only booking the houseboat.** The backwaters are incredible but Munnar, Fort Kochi, and the temples of Thrissur deserve equal time.
2. **Going in monsoon thinking it'll be empty.** Houseboat operations partly close June–August. Kerala in monsoon is beautiful but logistically harder.
3. **Eating at hotel restaurants.** The best Kerala food is at small "meals" joints (₹120 for unlimited Sadhya) and fish stalls along the backwater canals.
