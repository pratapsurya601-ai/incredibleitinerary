## The Problem With How Most People Do Goa

Goa has 101km of coastline. Most tourists see about 8km of it — the North Goa party strip between Baga and Vagator.

That 8km is legitimately fun for one evening. But spending 4–5 days there and calling it "Goa" is like spending 4 days in Times Square and claiming you know New York.

The real Goa requires a scooter, a willingness to get slightly lost, and the knowledge that the best beaches in Goa have almost no tourists on them.

## What 3 Ideal Goa Days Actually Look Like

### Day 1: North Goa Done Right

Not Baga. Not Calangute.

**Vagator Beach** — specifically the northern section (walk 10 minutes past the main beach to Ozran/Little Vagator). Dramatic red cliffs, smaller crowds, better swimming. Scramble up the cliff at the north end for views of the Chapora River estuary.

**Chapora Fort** — a crumbling Portuguese fort, 15-minute climb, free entry. The view from the top: two rivers, the Arabian Sea, fishing boats. Yes, it's the Dil Chahta Hai fort. It's also genuinely spectacular.

**Anjuna Flea Market** (Wednesdays) — the real one, not the tourist mall. Come at 9am. A Goan aunt selling prawn curry from a portable stall. A Kashmiri selling silver jewellery. An Israeli selling hand-stitched bags. This is the multicultural chaos that makes Goa strange and wonderful.

### Day 2: Old Goa (The Part Nobody Goes To)

This is the most underrated 4 hours in Goa.

**Old Goa** — a UNESCO site 30km from Anjuna. The Portuguese built a cathedral city here in the 16th century that was bigger than Lisbon. The Basilica of Bom Jesus holds the body of St Francis Xavier, preserved since 1552. The Sé Cathedral is Asia's largest. The churches are extraordinary and almost always quiet.

**Fontainhas, Panjim** — narrow lanes of yellow, ochre, and terracotta Portuguese houses. The most photogenic 30 minutes in Goa. Get a cup of Goan fish curry at Mahalaxmi Restaurant (since 1938) afterward.

**Feni tasting** — Goa's local cashew spirit. Strong. Acquired taste. One glass at a local bar near Panjim's riverside (₹60). You don't love it. You respect it.

### Day 3: South Goa (Where the Beach Actually Is)

**Palolem Beach** — the crescent beach. Best in India for swimming (calm water, no riptides unlike North Goa). Arrive before 8am to have it to yourself.

**Butterfly Beach** (boat from Palolem, ₹200) — accessible only by water. A cove. Perfect white sand. Almost never crowded. Go early.

**Agonda Beach** (15km north of Palolem) — wilder, longer, no development. Olive Ridley turtles nest here November–February.

**Cabo de Rama Fort** — a Portuguese cliff fort completely unknown to mass tourism. The views of the coastline from 200m up are the best in Goa.

## 5 Things That Ruin Goa Trips

**1. Calangute in December.** It's essentially a parking lot. The beach is fine but the approach is gridlocked.

**2. Not renting a scooter.** Ola/Uber surge pricing in Goa is brutal. You'll spend ₹800 on a trip that would have cost ₹60 on a scooter. Renting for 3 days costs ₹900 total.

**3. Only doing beaches.** Old Goa and Fontainhas are worth half a day each. Missing them means missing what makes Goa genuinely unique.

**4. Going to beach clubs that could be in Dubai.** LPK, SinQ, etc. — expensive, generic, excellent if that's your thing. Not Goa.

**5. Eating at strip restaurants.** Walk 200m off any main beach road. Prices drop 40%, quality doubles.

## The Question I Get Asked Most About Goa

"Is Goa worth it if I don't drink/party?"

Yes.

The beaches, the history, the food, the Portuguese architecture — none of this requires alcohol or nightlife. Goa's natural and cultural offerings are independently extraordinary. The party reputation is accurate for a specific geography (North Goa, specific beaches). The rest of the state is just beautiful.
