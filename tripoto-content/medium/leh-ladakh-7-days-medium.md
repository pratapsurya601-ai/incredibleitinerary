## The Altitude Reality (Not the Scary Version)

Leh sits at 3,524m above sea level. That's higher than the Zugspitze (Germany's highest peak). The air has about 65% of the oxygen available at sea level.

When you fly from Delhi (216m) to Leh (3,524m) in 70 minutes, your body has no idea what just happened. Altitude sickness — headaches, nausea, breathlessness, dizziness — affects about 40% of visitors to Leh in the first 24 hours.

**The protocol that works:**

**Day 1:** Land. Rest. Don't climb stairs quickly. Drink 4–5 litres of water. Eat light. No alcohol. If headache comes: paracetamol + rest. If breathlessness at rest or vomiting begins: doctor immediately.

**Day 2:** Gentle sightseeing only (short walks, Leh town). No Khardung La. No Pangong.

**Day 3 onward:** You've acclimatised. Go everywhere.

The people who get sick are the ones who go straight to the Gondola at Gulmarg (wrong mountain) — I mean the ones who land in Leh and immediately try to go to Khardung La the same afternoon. Don't.

## The Itinerary That Respects Altitude (And Still Sees Everything)

### Days 1–2: Leh Acclimatisation

**Leh market:** The main bazaar is a pleasant evening walk at pace. Turquoise jewellery, Tibetan thangkas, Ladakhi apricot jam (buy several jars — genuinely extraordinary), dried apricots from local farms. The market pace naturally limits exertion.

**Shanti Stupa (Day 2):** 5km from Leh, 500 steps up a hill. Take them slowly — genuinely slowly. The view from the top — snow peaks in every direction, Indus valley below, Leh town spread out — is one of the defining images of Ladakh.

**Leh Palace:** 9-storey 17th-century palace above the market. Modelled on Potala Palace in Lhasa. Partial ruin, completely magnificent.

**Sankar Monastery (2km walk, gentle):** A small active monastery, almost no tourists. Monks study here in the mornings. The main assembly hall has butter lamps and thankas. Free entry.

### Days 3–4: Nubra Valley

**The Khardung La crossing:** 5,359m. Drive from Leh (3.5 hours). Snow walls 4m high. Thin air — walking to the tea stall takes real effort. Stop briefly, take photos, continue (prolonged time at extreme altitude without movement is counterproductive for most people).

**Nubra Valley:** The descent from Khardung La to the Nubra Valley is one of the great drives in India. You go from moonscape to an unexpectedly green valley where the Shyok and Nubra rivers meet. Sand dunes appear. Bactrian camels graze.

**Hunder:** The famous double-humped camel ride on the cold desert. The backdrop of snow peaks + sand is the most surreal visual in India. ₹400 for a 45-minute ride.

**Diskit Monastery:** A 32m Maitreya Buddha facing north, toward Pakistan. The monastery perched on a cliff above the valley.

**Overnight in Nubra:** Tent camps or guesthouses in Hunder or Sumur — ₹1,200–₹3,500/night.

### Days 4–5: Pangong Lake

The drive from Nubra to Pangong Lake (via Wari La, 5 hours) is the most beautiful drive in India. I say this having done many drives people claim are the most beautiful in India.

**Pangong Tso:** 134km long, 60% in Tibet, sits at 4,350m. The colour shifts from turquoise to cobalt to violet depending on time of day and sky. The Tibetan plateau visible at the far end.

**Sunrise at Pangong:** This is the experience. Set an alarm for 5am. Walk to the shore before the sun appears. The mountains reflect on the still water. The sky turns violet, then orange, then that specific blue that doesn't exist anywhere else.

No photo I've seen does this justice. None.

**Overnight by the lake:** Luxury camps (₹8,000–₹15,000/person) or village guesthouses in Spangmik (₹1,500/person). The camps are overpriced. The experience of sleeping next to Pangong is not overpriced.

### Days 6–7: Return via Chang La + Monasteries

**Chang La Pass (5,360m):** Second of the high passes. Similar experience to Khardung La but less trafficked.

**Hemis Monastery:** The largest monastery in Ladakh. Built 1672. Extraordinary thangka collection (stored in museum vaults). During Hemis Festival (June–July): the most spectacular masked dance festival in the Himalayas.

**Thiksey Monastery:** Often called the "Potala of Ladakh." A tiered complex of white-washed buildings on a hillside. The 15m Maitreya statue inside. The view from the top over the Indus valley.

**Day 7:** Spituk Monastery (8km from airport, morning prayers at dawn if you're early) → departure.

## The Permits You Need

This is one of the most searched questions about Ladakh and most guides don't give complete information:

**Inner Line Permit (ILP):**
- Required for: Nubra Valley, Pangong Tso, Tso Moriri, Dha-Hanu
- Apply online: lahdclehpermit.nic.in (takes 2–3 hours to process)
- Cost: ₹400–₹600 depending on area
- Valid for specific dates — get it 1 day before you need it

**Protected Area Permit (PAP):**
- Required for foreign nationals (not Indian citizens)
- Apply at Leh DC Office with passport copies
- Can take a full day to process

**Tip:** Apply for your ILP the evening before you need it. The online portal is functional but sometimes slow.
