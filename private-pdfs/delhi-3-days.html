Day 1
🕌 Old Delhi — Mughal Heart
Shahjahanabad — the walled city built by Emperor Shah Jahan in 1639. Red Fort, the colossal Jama Masjid, the magnificent chaos of Chandni Chowk, paranthe wali gali (the lane of parathas), and Karim's — which has been feeding Delhi since 1913.
Day 2
🏛️ New Delhi — Colonial & Classical
Lutyens' Delhi: India Gate, Rajpath (now Kartavya Path), Humayun's Tomb (the prototype for the Taj Mahal), Qutub Minar (1193 AD, 72.5 metres), and Hauz Khas Village for the evening — ruins, a lake, cafes and boutiques in medieval walls.
Day 3
⛩️ Akshardham & Lodi Garden
Akshardham Temple — opened 2005, built without modern machinery, claims to be the world's largest Hindu temple complex (Guinness record). No phones permitted. Then Lodi Garden: 90 acres of manicured gardens around 15th-century tombs. Delhi's most beautiful park.
Throughout
🍽️ The Food Capital of India
Delhi debates with Mumbai for India's food crown — and wins on sheer variety. Old Delhi's Mughal-lineage kebabs and biryanis. Paranthe wali gali since the 1870s. CP's dhaba culture. South Delhi's café scene. Every cuisine of India is represented and done well.

"Delhi is not one city. It is eight cities stacked on top of each other, each empire leaving its ruins for the next to build upon. It is the most historically dense capital on earth."

Best Time to Visit: October to March. December and January are cool (8–20°C) and smoggy — the famous Delhi winter fog (and AQI above 300) is real; pack a mask if you're sensitive. February to March is perfect: clear, 18–28°C, gardens in bloom. April onwards gets brutal, topping 45°C in May–June. Monsoon (July–September) is hot and humid but clears the air significantly.

Delhi 3-Day Itinerary Day 1–2 — Old Delhi & New Delhi
01
Day One · Old Delhi
Red Fort, Jama Masjid & Chandni Chowk
Red Fort · Jama Masjid · Chandni Chowk food walk · Karim's for lunch · Spice market · Evening at Connaught Place
💰 Entries: ₹500 · Food: ₹300–₹600 · Transport: ₹100 metro
9:00 AM
Red Fort — Shah Jahan's Red Sandstone Masterpiece
Built in 1639–48, the Red Fort (Lal Qila) was the ceremonial and political heart of the Mughal Empire for 200 years. The two main palaces — Diwan-i-Aam (Hall of Public Audience) and Diwan-i-Khas (Hall of Private Audience) — are the finest surviving examples of Mughal palatial architecture. The famous Peacock Throne once stood in the Diwan-i-Khas, stolen by Nadir Shah in 1739. Prime Minister delivers Independence Day speech here every 15 August. Morning light on the red sandstone is spectacular.
Entry: ₹35 Indians · ₹500 foreigners · Metro to Chandni Chowk station (Yellow line) · Open 9am–5pm
11:00 AM
Jama Masjid — The Largest Mosque in India
Built by Shah Jahan between 1644–1656, Jama Masjid can accommodate 25,000 worshippers in its courtyard. The mosque is active — non-Muslims are welcome outside prayer times. The view from the south minaret (₹100) over Old Delhi and across to the Red Fort is one of Delhi's finest. Cover head and remove shoes (free to borrow at entrance). The mosque sits at the edge of Chandni Chowk market — leave immediately after visiting to start the food walk.
Entry: free · Minaret climb: ₹100 · Cover head — scarves available at entrance · Prayer times: avoid 12:30–1:30pm
12:00 PM
Chandni Chowk Food Walk — Paranthe Wali Gali & Jalebis
The main Chandni Chowk bazaar dates to the 17th century. Start at Paranthe Wali Gali (the lane of parathas) — three family-run shops (Pandit Gaya Prasad Shiv Charan and rivals) have served stuffed parathas since the 1870s. ₹80–₹120 for a full plate with curds and chutneys. Then walk to the jalebi vendor near Dariba Kalan for fresh jalebis in ghee (₹50/100g). The Spice Market (Khari Baoli — Asia's largest wholesale spice market) is nearby: the smell of cumin, turmeric and cardamom is overwhelming and magnificent.
Parathas: ₹80–₹120 · Jalebis: ₹50/100g · Spice market: free to browse
1:30 PM
Karim's — Feeding Delhi Since 1913
Tucked in a lane near Jama Masjid, Karim's was established in 1913 by the descendants of chefs from the Mughal imperial kitchen. The mutton burra kebab, seekh kebab and mutton nahari (slow-cooked overnight broth) are among the finest examples of Mughal cooking in India. It's not fancy — plastic chairs, fluorescent lights — but the food is historically significant and genuinely extraordinary. Vegetarians: the dal and paratha are excellent.
Meal per person: ₹300–₹500 · Address: 16 Jama Masjid Lane · Open for lunch and dinner
02
Day Two · New Delhi
India Gate, Humayun's Tomb & Qutub Minar
India Gate · Rashtrapati Bhavan · Humayun's Tomb · Qutub Minar · Hauz Khas Village evening
💰 Entries: ₹850 total · Hauz Khas dinner: ₹600–₹1,500
9:00 AM
India Gate & Kartavya Path — The Imperial Axis
India Gate, built in 1931, commemorates 82,000 Indian soldiers who died in World War I. The eternal flame (Amar Jawan Jyoti) burns beneath it. The tree-lined Kartavya Path (formerly Rajpath) runs from India Gate to Rashtrapati Bhavan — the grandest ceremonial avenue in any capital. Walk the 2km to Rashtrapati Bhavan — the President's residence is visible from the road. The Changing of the Guard ceremony is on Saturdays at 8am. Early morning is best; crowds build after 10am.
India Gate: free · Metro to Central Secretariat (Yellow/Violet line) · Bring water, very little shade
11:00 AM
Humayun's Tomb — The Prototype for the Taj Mahal
Built in 1572 by Humayun's widow, this was the first garden-tomb in the Indian subcontinent and directly inspired the Taj Mahal (built 73 years later). The geometry is perfect: a central tomb set in a Persian char-bagh (four-quadrant garden). The Aga Khan Trust's restoration work has been so thorough that the garden now looks as it did in the 16th century. Quieter and more contemplative than the Taj — and arguably more historically significant. Allow 1.5–2 hours.
Entry: ₹35 Indians · ₹550 foreigners · Metro to JLN Stadium · Open 6am–6pm
2:00 PM
Qutub Minar — India's Tallest Minaret, Built in 1193
At 72.5 metres, the Qutub Minar is the tallest brick minaret in the world, built by Qutb ud-Din Aibak after the defeat of the last Hindu kingdom of Delhi in 1193. The complex also contains the Quwwat-ul-Islam mosque — the first mosque built in India — and the Iron Pillar, a 7-metre column from the 4th–5th century AD that has not rusted in 1,600 years (the metallurgical sophistication of ancient India still puzzles scientists). No longer possible to climb the minaret (closed since 1981 due to a stampede), but the complex is extraordinary from ground level.
Entry: ₹35 Indians · ₹600 foreigners · Metro to Qutub Minar · 1–1.5 hours sufficient
Evening
Hauz Khas Village — Medieval Ruins Meet Rooftop Bars
One of Delhi's most unusual neighbourhoods: a 14th-century madrasa and royal tomb complex on the banks of a medieval reservoir, now surrounded by boutiques, cafes, galleries and rooftop restaurants. Walk through the ruins at sunset (free, accessible from the village lanes), then choose from dozens of restaurants. Social (₹600/person), Hauz Khas Social Bar (₹800/person with drinks), or the local dhabas on the street for ₹200 per person.
Ruins: free · Dinner: ₹400–₹1,500 · Auto or Uber from Qutub Minar: ₹150–₹200

Day Three · Temples & Gardens
Akshardham, Lotus Temple & Lodi Garden
Akshardham complex · Lotus Temple · Lodi Garden · Connaught Place shopping · Departure
💰 All entries free or low cost · Total day: ₹300–₹500 excluding shopping
9:30 AM
Akshardham Temple — No Phones, Pure Architecture
Opened in 2005 and holding a Guinness World Record for the world's largest comprehensive Hindu temple complex, Akshardham was built by 11,000 artisans and volunteers, using traditional methods without steel. The central monument in pink Rajasthani sandstone and white Carrara marble features 234 carved pillars, 9 ornate domes and 20,000 murti statues. No phones, cameras, bags, leather or food are permitted inside (all deposited in free lockers). The boat ride narrating 10,000 years of Indian history (₹173) and the IMAX show are excellent. Allow 3–4 hours.
Temple: free · Boat ride: ₹173 · IMAX: ₹80 · Metro to Akshardham (Blue line) · Open 9:30am–6:30pm
2:00 PM
Lotus Temple — Bahai House of Worship
The Lotus Temple, built in 1986, is one of the most visited buildings in the world — receiving more visitors annually than the Eiffel Tower. It belongs to the Bahai faith, which accepts all religions equally. The flower-shaped white marble structure with 27 petals arranged in clusters of three is architecturally extraordinary. Inside: silence, cool air, and a feeling of extraordinary calm. No religious ceremonies, no idols, no rituals — just meditation and silence. All faiths welcome. Metro to Kalkaji Mandir (Violet line).
Entry: free · Metro to Kalkaji Mandir · 30–45 minutes · Dress modestly
3:30 PM
Lodi Garden — 90 Acres of Tombs and Tranquility
Lodi Garden is Delhi's most beautiful park: 90 acres of manicured grounds containing 15th-century Sayyid and Lodi dynasty tombs, rose gardens and a bonsai park. In the morning and evening it fills with Delhi's joggers, meditators and families. The octagonal tomb of Muhammad Shah (1444) and the round-domed Sheesh Gundj are the most impressive structures. In February, the entire garden is in bloom. Virtually unknown to international tourists but beloved by Delhiites. 1 hour is sufficient.
Entry: free · Open sunrise to sunset · Metro to JLN Stadium or auto from Lotus Temple
Evening
Connaught Place — Shopping, Coffee & Last Delhi Bites
Connaught Place (CP) — Delhi's circular Georgian-style commercial hub from the 1930s — is still the city's social centre. The colonnaded inner and outer circles house bookshops, state emporiums (fixed-price handicrafts from every Indian state), restaurants and the famous Wenger's bakery (est. 1926 — try the chocolate pastry). For dinner, Pandara Road (a 5-minute auto) has Delhi's most reliable restaurant row: Ichiban for butter chicken, Havemore for kebabs. Close the evening with a chai at any street stall on CP's outer circle.
Shopping: free to browse · State emporiums for fixed-price handicrafts · Dinner at Pandara Road: ₹500–₹1,000

Delhi 3-Day Itinerary Insider Tips & Packing List
Insider Tips — Read Before You Go

Know Before You Go

🚇
Delhi Metro — Use It Religiously
Delhi's metro network is extensive, cheap (₹10–₹60), air-conditioned and punctual. It connects every major tourist site. The Airport Express Line (orange) goes from IGI Airport to New Delhi station in 19 minutes (₹60). Buy a stored-value smart card on arrival — no queue at token machines. Ladies-only coach is the first coach from the engine — enforce your right to it.
🍽️
Where to Eat — Beyond Karim's
Al Jawahar (next to Karim's, slightly cheaper, equally good). Natraj Dahi Bhalla at Chandni Chowk for the best dahi bhalla in India. Roshan Di Kulfi (Karol Bagh) for malai kulfi. Indian Coffee House at CP for cheap South Indian food in a heritage setting. Saravana Bhavan in CP for reliable South Indian. Kake Da Hotel (CP area) for butter chicken at ₹350.
🌫️
Delhi AQI — Be Prepared
October to February, Delhi's air quality can reach "severe" (AQI 400+). This is a real health concern, especially for children, elderly and those with respiratory conditions. Pack N95 masks (not surgical masks — they don't filter PM2.5). The AQI app or IQAir website gives real-time readings. Outdoor exercise is inadvisable when AQI exceeds 300. Indoor sites (Red Fort, Qutub, Humayun's Tomb) are fine as they're open-air but have trees.
🚫
Tourist Traps to Avoid
Auto and taxi drivers near Connaught Place who offer "free tours" that end at gem shops. Overpriced restaurants on the inner circle of CP (check the menu prices outside before entering). Fake government tourist offices near major hotels — only the India Tourism office at 88 Janpath is genuine. "Gem export business" — a classic Delhi scam. If anyone says you can make money reselling gems abroad: walk away.
🛍️
Shopping — What & Where
State Emporiums on Baba Kharak Singh Marg (near CP): fixed-price, quality-guaranteed handicrafts from every state. Dilli Haat (INA): open-air market of crafts from across India, ₹30 entry. Chandni Chowk for wholesale fabric, spices, silver. Lajpat Nagar for inexpensive ethnic clothing and jewellery. Avoid Kashmir "government-approved" shops that claim special deals — they are commission traps.
🕌
Mosque Etiquette — Jama Masjid
Jama Masjid is an active mosque. Non-Muslims are welcome outside the five daily prayer times (Fajr at dawn, Dhuhr 12:30pm, Asr 3:30pm, Maghrib at sunset, Isha at night). Remove shoes at the entrance (free to leave, or carry in a bag provided). Women must cover head and wear a long skirt or abaya (available to borrow free at the gate). Photography of worshippers without permission is disrespectful — don't do it.
🌡️
Seasons & When to Go
October–November: post-monsoon, pleasant, Diwali (festivals, lights, but also smoke). December–January: cold (5–18°C), fog, smog — carry a warm layer for mornings. February–March: perfect (18–28°C), clear skies, Holi in March. April onwards: increasingly hot. May–June: 42–48°C — visiting historic sites at noon is genuinely dangerous. Monsoon (July–September): humid but the gardens are magnificent and AQI drops dramatically.
🚕
Getting Around
Metro is cheapest and fastest for most tourist routes. Uber and Ola are reliable and metered app-based. Pre-paid auto counters at major metro stations give fixed-rate autos — use these instead of negotiating. Shared autos run fixed routes in residential areas — very cheap but confusing without Hindi. Cycle rickshaws are best for Old Delhi's narrow lanes where cars can't go.
Packing for Delhi
😷 N95 masks — non-negotiable in winter. Delhi's AQI regularly exceeds 300 in Dec–Jan. Pack at least 6.
🕌 Scarf/stole — for mosque visits, also doubles as a sun shield on Kartavya Path.
👟 Walking shoes — Qutub Minar, Humayun's Tomb and Lodi Garden involve extensive walking on uneven ground.
🧥 Warm layer — December/January mornings in Delhi are genuinely cold. 8°C at dawn is possible.
🔋 Power bank — a full day of metro navigation and Google Maps will drain any phone.
💊 Stomach meds — even veteran India travellers get Delhi Belly. Norfloxacin + Metronidazole, prescribed by a doctor before travel.
💵 Mixed cash — entry fees at monuments are cash-only at some counters. Keep ₹2,000 in small notes.
📱 Offline Google Maps — download Delhi offline before arriving. Metro routes, monuments, restaurants — all accessible without data.
🧴 Sunscreen + lip balm — the Delhi sun and dry air are both harsh. Even in winter the UV index is high.
One last thing: Delhi is overwhelming on Day 1. The traffic, the smog, the noise, the scale. Push through it. By Day 2 it begins to reveal its extraordinary layers — Mughal, British, modern — all simultaneously present in the same street. There is no city in India where you can stand in a 16th-century tomb, see a British colonial building, and hear a Bollywood ringtone — all at the same time.
