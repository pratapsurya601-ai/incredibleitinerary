Day 1–2
🌊 Yamunotri
Source of the Yamuna river. A 6km trek from Janki Chatti (1,400m) to the temple (3,293m). The Divya Shila rock pillar and the hot springs (Surya Kund) where pilgrims cook rice. One of the least crowded of the four dhams. Truly remote and spiritually powerful.
Day 3
🏔️ Gangotri
Origin of the Ganga river. The temple sits at 3,048m on the banks of the Bhagirathi river. Option to trek 19km (return) to Gaumukh — the glacier snout where the Ganga actually emerges. The landscape here is extraordinary — high Himalayan drama with the snow-covered Bhagirathi peaks towering above.
Day 4–5
⛰️ Kedarnath
One of the 12 Jyotirlinga shrines of Lord Shiva, at 3,583m. Reached by a 16km trek from Gaurikund. The stone temple survived the 2013 floods that killed thousands — the Shiva lingam considered miraculous. The landscape is dramatic Himalayan tundra. Optional helicopter (₹5,000–₹8,000 one way).
Day 6–7
🙏 Badrinath
Dedicated to Lord Vishnu, at 3,133m. One of the Char Dham of all India (the others being Dwarka, Puri, Rameswaram). Tapt Kund — hot spring for ritual bathing. The colourful temple facade is instantly recognisable. Nearby: Mana village (last Indian village before Tibet), Vasudhara Falls.

"The Char Dham is not a trek — it is a transformation. The roads test you. The altitude humbles you. The temples receive you exactly as you are."

Registration is MANDATORY: Register free at devasthanam.uk.gov.in before departure. Carry a government photo ID. Fill the health declaration form. Registration points also available at Haridwar and Rishikesh for those without internet access. Do NOT attempt to enter without registration — checkposts verify documents.
Season Warning: Roads open May–June and September–October ONLY. They close during monsoon (July–August — landslides) and winter (November–April — snow). Confirm current road/temple status before departure at uttarakhandtourism.gov.in. Weather can change without warning.

Char Dham Yatra — 7-Day Itinerary Days 1–4 — Yamunotri, Gangotri, Kedarnath Approach
01
Day One · Rishikesh to Barkot
The Journey Begins — Into Uttarakhand
Haridwar/Rishikesh → Barkot (210km, 6–7hrs) · Register on Char Dham portal · Rest and acclimatise
💰 Shared vehicle: ₹400–600 · Private car: ₹3,000–4,000 · Stay Barkot: ₹600–2,000
Morning
Rishikesh/Haridwar → Barkot via Mussoorie and Purola
The drive from Rishikesh to Barkot (base camp for Yamunotri) takes 6–7 hours through beautiful Garhwal hill country. The road climbs steadily into the mountains with views of the Himalayan foothills opening up. Stop at Mussoorie (90km) for chai and mountain views if time allows. Barkot sits at ~1,200m — altitude is manageable. Register on devasthanam.uk.gov.in if not already done. Carry all documents.
Distance: 210km · Time: 6–7hrs · Shared SUV from Rishikesh: ₹400–600/seat
Evening
Barkot — Rest, Prepare, Acclimatise
Barkot is a small hill town with adequate guesthouses and dharamshalas. Eat a light meal — high altitude pilgrimages require staying hydrated and not overeating. Avoid alcohol completely during the yatra — altitude and alcohol are a dangerous combination. Pack your day bag for tomorrow's trek: water, glucose biscuits, light snacks, warm layer (temperature at Yamunotri temple can drop to 5°C even in June), rain poncho, sturdy walking shoes.
Dharamshalas: ₹200–₹500 · Budget guesthouses: ₹600–₹1,200
02
Day Two · First Dham
Yamunotri — Source of the Sacred Yamuna
Barkot → Janki Chatti (30km) → Trek 6km to Yamunotri temple → return → drive to Uttarkashi
💰 Pony: ₹600–₹800 one way · Doli: ₹1,200–₹1,500 · Pooja at temple: ₹100–₹500
6:00 AM
Drive to Janki Chatti — Trek Base
Janki Chatti (1,400m) is where the motorable road ends. From here it's a 6km trek (mostly gradual climb) through beautiful forest and beside a stream to the Yamunotri temple (3,293m). The walk takes 2–3 hours one way. Pony and doli (palanquin) services are available at Janki Chatti for those who need them. The trail is well-marked and used by thousands of pilgrims daily during season. The last 1km is steeper.
Trek: 6km one way · Time: 2–3hrs up · Pony: ₹600–₹800 one way · Doli: ₹1,200–₹1,500
9:00 AM
Yamunotri Temple — Darshan & Hot Springs
The Yamunotri temple houses an idol of Goddess Yamuna in black marble. Before entering, pilgrims bathe in the Surya Kund — a boiling hot spring where you can cook rice (bring raw rice, it will cook in 10 minutes, this is then prasad). The Divya Shila — a sacred rock pillar — receives puja before temple entry. Darshan queues are manageable compared to Kedarnath or Badrinath. The setting — surrounded by high mountains — is magnificent.
Temple entry: free · Pooja: ₹100–₹500 · Bring raw rice for Surya Kund cooking
Afternoon
Trek Back + Drive to Uttarkashi
Return trek to Janki Chatti (2hrs), then drive 100km to Uttarkashi — a larger town on the Bhagirathi river with better accommodation options than Barkot. Uttarkashi is itself a pilgrimage town with the Vishwanath Temple and the Nehru Institute of Mountaineering. Overnight here.
Distance Janki Chatti to Uttarkashi: 100km · 3hrs
03
Day Three · Second Dham
Gangotri — Where the Ganga Descends
Uttarkashi → Gangotri (100km, 3hrs) · Temple darshan · Option: Gaumukh glacier trek (19km return)
💰 Gaumukh trek permit: ₹200 · Overnight tent at Bhojbasa: ₹500–₹800
7:00 AM
Uttarkashi → Gangotri — Mountain Road
The 100km drive to Gangotri (3,048m) takes 3 hours on a winding road through increasingly dramatic Himalayan terrain. The Bhagirathi river accompanies the road. Pass through Harsil — a stunning village often called the most beautiful in Uttarakhand, surrounded by apple orchards and deodar cedar forests. Gangotri town is compact — a few hundred metres of dharamshalas and shops leading to the temple.
Distance: 100km · 3hrs drive · Harsil stop recommended (30 min)
10:00 AM
Gangotri Temple Darshan
The bright white temple dedicated to Goddess Ganga sits directly on the banks of the Bhagirathi. Pilgrims take a ritual dip in the icy Bhagirathi waters (18°C) before darshan — the cold is intense but the experience is transcendental. The temple sanctum is relatively small — morning queues can be long in peak season (June, September). The confluence of ice-cold mountain air, river sound, chanting and incense creates an atmosphere unlike anywhere else in India.
Temple: free · Ritual dip in Bhagirathi: free · Pooja: ₹50–₹500
Afternoon
Gaumukh Trek Option — The Glacier Snout (19km Return)
For the physically fit and adventurous: the Gaumukh trek (19km return from Gangotri) leads to the actual snout of the Gangotri Glacier — where the Bhagirathi/Ganga physically emerges from the ice. Permit required (₹200). The trek takes 6–7 hours return through alpine meadows and moraines. Overnight at Bhojbasa ashram/tents is possible. This is one of the finest high-altitude treks in India. Not required for darshan — optional extension for trekkers.
Gaumukh permit: ₹200 · Time: 6–7hrs return · Bhojbasa tent: ₹500–₹800/night
04
Day Four · Long Drive
Gangotri to Guptkashi — The Road to Kedarnath
220km drive (7–8hrs) via Uttarkashi and Tehri · Arrive Guptkashi or Sonprayag · Rest
💰 Longest driving day · Private car strongly recommended · Stay Guptkashi: ₹800–₹3,000
Full Day
The Long Drive — Gangotri to Guptkashi (220km, 7–8hrs)
This is the longest driving day of the yatra, crossing from one Himalayan valley to another. The route passes through Uttarkashi, then descends to Tehri Dam (one of Asia's tallest dams, visible from the road — reservoir stretches for miles), then climbs again through Srinagar Garhwal (not to be confused with Kashmir's Srinagar) and up to Guptkashi or Sonprayag. Very scenic but exhausting. Depart by 6am. Take breaks but don't dawdle — mountain roads close at dusk in poor weather.
Distance: 220km · Time: 7–8hrs · Dehri Dam view: noteworthy stop
Evening
Guptkashi — Vishwanath Temple & Registration for Kedarnath
Guptkashi has a small Vishwanath temple and the Ardh-Narishwar temple worth visiting. Confirm Kedarnath registration is complete. Check weather and route conditions for tomorrow — the Sonprayag–Gaurikund road and trek conditions can change overnight due to weather. Eat well tonight; tomorrow's trek is demanding. Organise porter or horse booking through your accommodation if needed.
Guptkashi guesthouses: ₹800–₹2,500 · Dharamshalas: ₹200–₹500

Char Dham Yatra — 7-Day Itinerary Days 5–7 — Kedarnath, Badrinath & Return
05
Day Five · Third Dham
Kedarnath — The Jyotirlinga at 3,583m
Sonprayag → Gaurikund jeep → 16km trek → Kedarnath darshan · Overnight or return to valley
💰 Trek: free · Helicopter: ₹5,000–₹8,000 one way · Temple pooja: ₹500–₹1,500
5:00 AM
Sonprayag → Gaurikund (5km by Shared Jeep)
Private vehicles cannot proceed beyond Sonprayag — shared jeeps (₹30–₹50) take you the final 5km to Gaurikund (1,982m), where the trek begins. Gaurikund has a hot spring where pilgrims take a ritual dip before the trek. Cloakroom facilities available — leave heavy bags here. The trek begins at the checkpost where your registration is verified. Have all documents ready.
Sonprayag to Gaurikund: ₹30–₹50 shared jeep · Gaurikund hot spring: free
6:00 AM
Trek: Gaurikund to Kedarnath — 16km, 5–7 Hours
The trek route: Gaurikund (1,982m) → Jungle Chatti (4km) → Bheembali (7km) → Linchauri (10km) → Kedarnath (3,583m, 16km). The gradient is consistent but not extreme — the altitude gain of 1,600m is what makes it challenging. Pony: ₹2,500–₹3,500 one way. Doli/palanquin: ₹5,000–₹6,000. Helicopter from Phata/Sirsi: ₹5,000–₹8,000 one way (book on the official Uttarakhand helicopter booking portal — fills up fast in season). First-time trekkers should pace themselves — do not rush.
Pony: ₹2,500–₹3,500 · Doli: ₹5,000–₹6,000 · Helicopter: ₹5,000–₹8,000 · Trek: free
1:00 PM
Kedarnath Temple Darshan — The Shiva Lingam
The Kedarnath temple is one of India's most extraordinary religious structures — an ancient stone temple at 3,583m, surrounded by the Kedarnath, Kedar Dome and Bharatekuntha peaks. The temple survived the catastrophic 2013 flood that killed 5,000+ people, considered miraculous. The massive Shiva lingam inside is a naturally occurring rock formation — pilgrims pour milk, water and flowers on it. An early morning Abhishek darshan (5am, limited seats, ₹1,500) is the most powerful way to experience the temple.
Temple: free · Abhishek darshan: ₹1,500 (5am, book ahead) · Bhairon Temple: 500m further, worth visiting
06
Day Six · Fourth Dham
Badrinath — Lord Vishnu's Abode
Return to valley → Drive to Badrinath (200km, 5–6hrs via Joshimath) · Tapt Kund · Darshan
💰 Tapt Kund: free · Temple: free · Accommodation Badrinath: ₹500–₹3,000
Morning
Trek Down and Drive to Badrinath
Return trek from Kedarnath (3–4hrs) or helicopter back to Phata/Sirsi. Then drive to Badrinath (200km, 5–6hrs) via Guptkashi → Rudraprayag → Joshimath. The route is spectacular — the Alaknanda river gorge, the confluence at Rudraprayag (where Alaknanda meets Mandakini), the old town of Joshimath (important base for the Nanda Devi zone). Arrive Badrinath by evening.
Distance: 200km via Joshimath · 5–6hrs · Stop at Joshimath for fuel/food
Evening
Tapt Kund Hot Spring + Badrinath Temple Darshan
Tapt Kund is the hot sulphur spring at Badrinath where pilgrims bathe before entering the temple — it is genuinely hot (45°C) and luxurious after days of cold mountain travel. The Badrinath temple itself is colourfully painted (unlike most North Indian temples) — red, yellow and grey — and lit dramatically at night. Evening darshan (Sandhya Aarti at 8:30pm) is particularly beautiful. The presiding deity is Lord Vishnu in a meditating posture (Shri Badrinarayan).
Tapt Kund: free · Temple: free · Sandhya Aarti: 8:30pm daily · Arrive by 6pm
07
Day Seven · Last India Before Tibet
Mana Village, Vasudhara & the Return Journey
Mana village (3km from Badrinath) · Vasudhara Falls · Drive back to Rishikesh (300km)
💰 Mana village: free · Vasudhara Falls: 5km trek · Return drive: 6–8hrs
7:00 AM
Mana Village — The Last Indian Village
Mana (3,200m), just 3km from Badrinath, is traditionally considered the last Indian village before the Tibet border. It is inhabited by the Bhotiya tribe — their homes, temples, and way of life have barely changed for centuries. Vyasa Gufa (the cave where sage Vyasa wrote the Mahabharata — mythologically significant), Ganesh Gufa, and the roaring Saraswati river that disappears underground. Local women sell woollen items and Tibetan goods. One of the most atmospheric small villages in the Himalayas.
Mana Village: 3km from Badrinath · Free · Local woolens and handicrafts to buy
9:00 AM
Vasudhara Falls — 122m Waterfall in the Alpine Zone
A 5km trek from Mana village to Vasudhara Falls — a stunning 122-metre waterfall in a high alpine meadow setting. The water is icy cold. Legend says the water falls only on the pure-hearted — it curves around sinners (which is of course not literally true, but the saying creates a wonderful atmosphere of reflection). The landscape beyond Mana into the alpine zone is extraordinary. Return to Mana and begin the long drive back to Rishikesh.
Vasudhara Falls: 5km trek one way · Free · 3hrs round trip
Noon
Drive Back to Rishikesh (300km, 7–8hrs)
The return drive from Badrinath to Rishikesh (300km) takes 7–8 hours. Stop at Devprayag — the confluence of the Bhagirathi and Alaknanda rivers where the Ganga is officially named. The point where two rivers meet, visibly different in colour (grey and green), is one of the most beautiful natural sights in Uttarakhand. Arrive Rishikesh by evening. The Ganga Aarti at Haridwar or Rishikesh — a fitting conclusion to the yatra.
Distance: 300km · 7–8hrs · Stop at Devprayag (30min) · Triveni Ghat Aarti: 6pm

Char Dham Yatra — 7-Day Itinerary Insider Tips & Essential Information
Insider Tips — Read Before You Go

Know Before You Begin the Yatra

📋
Registration Is Non-Negotiable
Register at devasthanam.uk.gov.in before departing. Free, takes 10 minutes. You need Aadhaar or passport details, a photo, and a health declaration. Checkposts at multiple points on the route verify this — you cannot enter without it. Children under 12 and seniors over 75 need a medical clearance certificate from a government hospital.
🏥
Altitude Sickness — Take It Seriously
Kedarnath (3,583m) and Badrinath (3,133m) are high altitude. Acclimatise 1 night in Rishikesh or Haridwar before ascending rapidly. Symptoms: headache, nausea, dizziness, shortness of breath. Descend immediately if symptoms worsen. Diamox (acetazolamide) can help — consult your doctor before travel. Stay well-hydrated. Never rush ascent. Deaths occur on the Char Dham every season from cardiac events and altitude sickness.
📱
Helicopter Booking — Book Weeks Ahead
Kedarnath helicopter bookings open months before the season on the official IRCTC/Uttarakhand helicopter portal. Seats sell out within days of booking opening. If you plan to use helicopter service, set a calendar reminder and book the moment booking opens. Prices: ₹5,000–₹8,000 per person one way from Phata, Sirsi or Guptkashi helipads. Helicopters don't fly in bad weather.
🌧️
Monsoon Roads — Genuinely Dangerous
Do NOT travel the Char Dham circuit in July–August. Landslides block roads, sometimes for days. Flash floods occur without warning. The 2013 Kedarnath disaster (5,000+ deaths) happened in June during early monsoon. The governments now close roads proactively during heavy rain. Check uttarakhandtourism.gov.in and local news daily during your trip even in the official open season.
💰
Cash is King on This Circuit
Beyond Uttarkashi and Guptkashi, ATMs are rare and often out of service. Carry sufficient cash (at least ₹10,000 per person) before entering the circuit. Cards are accepted at few places beyond major town hotels. Pony operators, dhaba food stalls, portage, and most local services are cash-only.
🐴
Ponies — Book at Trek Start, Not Before
Don't book ponies through advance agents — rates are fixed at the official counters at Janki Chatti (Yamunotri) and Gaurikund (Kedarnath). The government sets rates. Anyone offering advance bookings in Rishikesh or online is likely adding a middleman commission. Book at the official counters on the day.
🌡️
What to Wear
Even in May and June, temperatures at Kedarnath and Badrinath drop to 2–8°C at night. It can snow in June. Carry thermals, a warm mid-layer, a windproof/waterproof outer jacket, and wool socks. Many pilgrims underestimate the cold and suffer. A ₹300 rain poncho from any mountain town is essential.
🙏
Temple Etiquette
Dress modestly — traditional Indian attire preferred but clean western clothes are acceptable if covering shoulders and legs. Remove footwear at the temple entrance. Do not carry leather items inside. Photography restricted or prohibited inside all four temple sanctums. Avoid eating non-vegetarian food during the yatra — the dhams are sattvic zones and it is respectful to the pilgrimage tradition.
Packing for Char Dham
🧥 Thermal inner layers — top and bottom. Even in June, nights are 2–8°C at altitude.
🌧️ Rain poncho — ₹200–300 from any hill station. Non-negotiable.
👟 Trek shoes — waterproof sole, ankle support. No sandals for the treks.
💊 Basic medical kit — ORS, paracetamol, antacid, glucose biscuits, Diamox (consult doctor).
💧 Water bottle 1L+ — refill at mountain streams/taps. Stay hydrated at altitude.
📄 ID copies x3 — Aadhaar/passport. Keep originals and copies separately.
🔦 Headlamp with batteries — early morning starts. Power cuts common in remote areas.
💰 Cash ₹10,000+ — ATMs rare. Bring from city before entering circuit.
🧻 Dry fruits & energy bars — nutrition on long trek days. Local trail mix available too.
