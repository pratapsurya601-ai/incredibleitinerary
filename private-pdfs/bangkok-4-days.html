💱 Currency & Money
Thai Baht (THB). 1 THB ≈ ₹2.4. So ₹100 = ~42 THB. Exchange at Superrich money changers (yellow/green) — best rates in Bangkok. Avoid airport forex counters (terrible rates). ATMs charge ₹500–₹600 per withdrawal — bring cash from India to exchange.
📱 SIM Card
Buy at Suvarnabhumi Airport arrivals hall. AIS Tourist SIM — 15 days unlimited data + 100 min calls: ~600 THB (≈₹1,450). DTAC is slightly cheaper at ~500 THB. Both work excellently across Bangkok. Your Indian number will rack up roaming charges — swap immediately on arrival.
🚇 Getting Around
BTS Skytrain: Fastest, AC, covers tourist areas. ~20–50 THB (₹48–₹120) per trip. MRT: Underground, similar pricing. Grab: Use always — fixed prices, AC, no scams. Never negotiate with metered taxis (tourist traps, often broken meters). River boats: Chao Phraya Express ₹30–75 (30 THB) — scenic & cheap.
🛂 Visa for Indians
Visa on Arrival — Free 30 days as of 2025 (India–Thailand bilateral agreement). Carry: passport (6+ months validity), return ticket, hotel booking, ₹5,000 in cash or equivalent THB proof of funds. Immigration is quick. No pre-application needed — just queue at the VOA counter at Suvarnabhumi.

Bangkok 4-Day Itinerary Day 1 — Old Bangkok
01
Day One · Rattanakosin District
Old Bangkok — Grand Palace, Temples & Khao San Road
Grand Palace · Wat Pho · Wat Arun · Chao Phraya River · Khao San Road evening
💰 Budget: ₹2,200–₹3,000 · Couple: ₹4,500–₹6,000 (excl. accommodation)
8:30 AM
Grand Palace — Arrive Right at Opening
The most stunning complex in Thailand and one of the most impressive in Asia. Home to the sacred Emerald Buddha. Dress code strictly enforced — shoulders and knees covered (sarongs available at gate). Allow 2–2.5 hours minimum. Grab from your hotel, arrive exactly at 8:30am to beat the crowd — by 10am it is packed. The Emerald Buddha chamber, Phra Mondop library, and the Grand Palace buildings are all within the compound.
Entry: 500 THB ≈ ₹1,200 | Open: 8:30am–3:30pm
11:00 AM
Wat Pho — The Reclining Buddha (10-min walk)
One of Bangkok's oldest and largest temples. Home to a 46-metre gilded reclining Buddha — the soles of the feet are inlaid with mother-of-pearl depicting the 108 characteristics of the Buddha. Also the birthplace of traditional Thai massage — get a 30-min foot massage in the temple grounds (300 THB ≈ ₹720, excellent value). Beautiful courtyard with dozens of smaller chedis.
Entry: 100 THB ≈ ₹240 | Massage: 300 THB ≈ ₹720
12:30 PM
Chao Phraya River Boat to Wat Arun
Walk to Pier 9 (Tha Tien, 5 minutes from Wat Pho). Take the cross-river ferry to Wat Arun — Temple of Dawn. One of Bangkok's most iconic skylines. Climb the steep prangs (towers) for views across the river. The mosaic-covered spires shimmer differently in morning vs afternoon light. Ferry is dirt cheap and part of the experience.
Ferry: 5 THB ≈ ₹12 each way | Wat Arun entry: 50 THB ≈ ₹120
2:30 PM
Chao Phraya Express Boat Upriver (Scenic + Cheap)
Take the orange-flag express boat from Wat Arun pier northward — pass under bridges, see temples from the water, spot longtail boats. Hop off at Phra Arthit pier near Khao San Road. The river is Bangkok's original highway and this 30-minute ride shows you a side of the city most tourists miss entirely.
Express boat: 15–30 THB ≈ ₹36–₹72
Evening
Khao San Road — Bangkok's Backpacker Hub
The most famous backpacker street in the world. Lively from 6pm, electric from 9pm. Street stalls sell pad thai (50 THB ≈ ₹120), fresh fruit, pad kra pao, insects (try if adventurous). Cheap beer from buckets (200 THB ≈ ₹480 for a bucket), live music everywhere. Great people-watching — mix of Thai locals, travellers from every country, and vendors. Walk the full length and nearby Rambuttri Road (quieter, better restaurants).
Beer: 80–120 THB ≈ ₹192–₹290 | Pad Thai: 50 THB ≈ ₹120
River tip: Buy a one-day pass for the Chao Phraya Tourist Boat (200 THB ≈ ₹480) if you plan multiple stops — it covers all the major piers including Grand Palace, Wat Pho, Wat Arun, Asiatique, and more. Unlimited hops all day.

Bangkok 4-Day Itinerary Day 2 — Modern Bangkok & Shopping
02
Day Two · Chatuchak · Siam · Silom
Modern Bangkok — Mega Markets, Malls & Rooftop Bars
Chatuchak Weekend Market · Jim Thompson House · MBK · Siam Paragon · Silom night market · Rooftop bar
💰 Budget: ₹2,500–₹4,000 · Couple: ₹6,000–₹12,000 (excl. accommodation)
Chatuchak Weekend Only: This market runs Saturdays and Sundays only, 9am–6pm. If you're visiting on a weekday, swap Day 2 and Day 3 activities. Missing Chatuchak is genuinely one of Bangkok's biggest tourist regrets.
9:00 AM
Chatuchak Weekend Market — Arrive Early
One of the world's largest markets — 15,000+ stalls across 35 acres. Clothes, vintage, furniture, antiques, plants, street food, ceramics, handmade goods, electronics accessories. Arrive by 9am before the heat and the 200,000 daily visitors turn it into a sweat-box by noon. Get a map at the entrance. Key sections: Section 2–4 (clothing), Section 7–8 (vintage), Section 26 (food). Bargaining is expected — start at 50% of asking price, settle around 65–70%.
Free entry | BTS Mo Chit or MRT Chatuchak Park | ₹0 entry
12:30 PM
Jim Thompson House — Silk Museum & Heritage House
An American silk entrepreneur who made Thai silk famous worldwide — and then mysteriously disappeared in the Malaysian jungle in 1967. His converted traditional Thai house is now a museum of Asian antiques, silk, and intrigue. Guided tours run every 20 minutes. The shop sells excellent quality silk products at fair prices. Near BTS National Stadium.
Entry: 200 THB ≈ ₹480 | Guided tour included | Open daily 9am–6pm
2:30 PM
MBK Center — Budget Shopping + AC Break
Seven-floor shopping mall, Bangkok's budget shopping destination. Electronics (good prices, check carefully), phone accessories, clothes, bags, shoes. Ground floor has a huge food court (cheap, local food, ₹150–₹300 per meal). Great place to stock up on gifts — phone cases, Thai snacks, keychains. Then walk across to Siam Paragon for a proper AC break — luxury brands, Kinokuniya bookstore, massive aquarium in the basement.
Free entry | Food court meals: 60–100 THB ≈ ₹145–₹240
6:30 PM
Silom Road Evening & Night Market
Silom is Bangkok's financial district by day, lively street scene by night. The Silom night market (near Sala Daeng BTS) has street food, clothes and accessories. Patpong Night Market nearby for souvenir shopping (bargain hard). The entire area comes alive at dusk — lots of dining options from cheap Thai to international restaurants.
Free to explore | Street food: 50–150 THB ≈ ₹120–₹360
8:30 PM
Rooftop Bar — Splurge or Budget Option
Lebua Sky Bar (The Hangover Bar): 64th floor, State Tower, Silom — the iconic bar from The Hangover Part II. Cocktails from 600 THB ≈ ₹1,440. Smart dress required. Reservations recommended. Utterly spectacular views. Budget option — Octave Rooftop Lounge (Marriott, Sukhumvit): Equally stunning panoramic views, cocktails from 350 THB ≈ ₹840, more relaxed dress code. Both are extraordinary at sunset.
Lebua: cocktails from 600 THB ≈ ₹1,440 | Octave: from 350 THB ≈ ₹840

Bangkok 4-Day Itinerary Day 3 — Day Trip
03
Day Three · Day Trip Options
Day Trip — Ayutthaya Ancient Capital or Floating Markets
Option A: Ayutthaya ruins by bicycle · Option B: Damnoen Saduak or Amphawa Floating Market
💰 Budget: ₹1,500–₹2,500 · Couple: ₹3,000–₹5,000 (excl. accommodation)
Option A — Ayutthaya (Recommended)
Why Ayutthaya: UNESCO World Heritage Site — once the richest city in Asia, now a landscape of ancient chedis and headless Buddhas. The iconic "Buddha head entwined in tree roots" at Wat Mahathat is here. More rewarding than the floating market for history lovers.
7:00 AM
Train from Hua Lamphong Station (MRT: Hua Lamphong)
Take the MRT to Hua Lamphong station (Bangkok's main railway terminus). Trains to Ayutthaya depart frequently from 6am. Journey: 1.5–2 hours. Third-class train: 20 THB ≈ ₹48. Second class: 60 THB ≈ ₹145. No booking needed — just buy at the window. The train runs through the heart of Thailand's central plains — rice paddies, temples, rural life.
Train: 20–60 THB ≈ ₹48–₹145 each way | ~1.5 hrs
9:00 AM
Hire Bicycle at Ayutthaya Station
Rent a bicycle right outside the station from multiple shops — the best way to cover the island city at your own pace. The entire ancient capital sits on an island surrounded by rivers. Distance between major sites: 1–3km. Cycling through temple ruins at dawn light with almost nobody around is one of Thailand's finest experiences. Alternatively, hire a tuk-tuk for the day (400–500 THB ≈ ₹960–₹1,200) if cycling isn't your preference.
Bicycle: 50–80 THB ≈ ₹120–₹192 per day
9:30 AM
Wat Mahathat — Buddha Head in Tree Roots
The most photographed image in Ayutthaya — a stone Buddha head entwined within the roots of a bodhi tree. How it got there is debated. Come early before the tour groups arrive. The surrounding temple complex is also beautiful — rows of headless Buddha statues, crumbling prangs covered in moss. Kneel when photographing the Buddha head as a sign of respect (it's required).
Entry: 50 THB ≈ ₹120
11:00 AM
Wat Phra Si Sanphet — Three Royal Chedis
The most impressive temple in Ayutthaya — three large bell-shaped chedis (stupas) in a row, containing the ashes of three Ayutthaya kings. Once the holiest temple in the kingdom, housing a 16-metre gold-plated standing Buddha (looted by Burmese invaders in 1767). The chedis are perfectly photogenic at any time of day. Adjacent is the Viharn Phra Mongkol Bophit — a large Buddha shrine, free entry.
Entry: 50 THB ≈ ₹120
3:00 PM
Return Train to Bangkok
Head back to the station by 3–4pm to catch the return train. Arrive in Bangkok around 5:30–6pm. Have dinner at Yaowarat (Chinatown) or near your hotel. Trains run until evening but it's nicer to arrive before dark.
Return train: 20–60 THB ≈ ₹48–₹145
Option B — Floating Markets
Damnoen Saduak
2 hrs from Bangkok · Daily · Go before 9am
The classic floating market — wooden boats loaded with tropical fruits, noodles, souvenirs. Very touristy but visually spectacular. Go early (7am departure from Bangkok) before tour groups arrive.
Amphawa Floating Market
90 km from Bangkok · Weekends only · More local
More authentic than Damnoen Saduak — frequented by Thai locals, not just tourists. Best on Friday–Sunday evenings. Fireflies on the river at night. Combine with Maeklong Railway Market (trains pass through a market stall — vendors fold awnings as the train approaches).

Bangkok 4-Day Itinerary Day 4 — Temples & Departure
04
Day Four · Wat Saket · Chinatown · Airport
Final Day — Golden Mount, Chinatown & Last Shopping
Wat Saket · Giant Swing · Amulet Market · Yaowarat Chinatown lunch · ICONSIAM · Suvarnabhumi
💰 Budget: ₹2,000–₹3,000 · Couple: ₹4,000–₹8,000 (excl. accommodation)
8:30 AM
Wat Saket — The Golden Mount (Best City Views)
A man-made hill topped with a golden chedi — Bangkok's only "mountain." Climb the 318 steps via a shaded spiral path past bells, prayer flags and small shrines. At the top, 360-degree panoramic views over Old Bangkok — the Royal Palace dome, Wat Pho, Wat Arun, and the modern city skyline all visible from one spot. Best visited at 8–9am before heat. Very few tourists this early. The climb takes 15 minutes and is completely worth it.
Entry: 20 THB ≈ ₹48 | Open from 7am
10:00 AM
Giant Swing & Amulet Market (Free)
A 5-minute walk from Wat Saket. The Giant Swing (Sao Ching Cha) is a massive red ceremonial swing used in Brahmin festivals — now a Bangkok landmark. Walk down to the nearby Amulet Market along Maharat Road (near Wat Mahathat on the Bangkok side). Hundreds of vendors sell Buddhist amulets, protective talismans and antique religious items. The atmosphere is fascinating — serious collectors haggling with monks. Completely free to browse.
Free | Open daily, mornings best
12:00 PM
Yaowarat — Bangkok's Chinatown for Lunch
Bangkok's Chinatown is one of the best in Southeast Asia. The main street (Yaowarat Road) is lined with gold shops, herbal medicine dealers, and the most incredible street food. For lunch: T&K Seafood for grilled prawns and oyster omelette (very popular, queue expected), or duck noodle soup from the countless small shophouses (80 THB ≈ ₹192). Dim sum at Shangarila: best roast duck and dim sum in Bangkok, ₹200–₹400 per person. Yaowarat is also famous for mango sticky rice dessert — multiple stalls, all excellent.
Meal: 100–200 THB ≈ ₹240–₹480 per person
2:30 PM
ICONSIAM — Last Shopping Stop
One of Bangkok's newest and most spectacular malls on the Chao Phraya riverfront. Take a river boat or Grab. Inside is the extraordinary SookSiam — an indoor floating market replicating Thailand's regional markets and crafts. Great for last-minute gift shopping: Thai silk, ceramics, dried fruits, packaged snacks. Also has a huge supermarket for picking up Thai curry pastes, Tom Yum packs and snacks to bring back to India. The riverside view from the upper floors is beautiful.
Free entry | BTS Gold Line to ICONSIAM
5:00 PM
Suvarnabhumi Airport — Arrive 3 Hours Early
Bangkok's main international airport. From central Bangkok (Siam/Sukhumvit): Airport Rail Link (ARL) from Phaya Thai station — 30 mins, 45 THB ≈ ₹108, runs every 15 mins. Or Grab for 400–600 THB ≈ ₹960–₹1,440 depending on traffic. Arrive 3 hours before departure — security queues can be long especially at evening peak. The duty-free at Suvarnabhumi is excellent — Thai whisky, cosmetics, snacks, silk. Pay in THB at the best rate.
ARL: 45 THB ≈ ₹108 | Grab: 400–600 THB ≈ ₹960–₹1,440
Airport shopping tip: Buy Chatuchak-priced Thai snacks and packaged goods from 7-Eleven or Family Mart in the city before heading to the airport — airport prices are 2–3x higher. Pack in your checked bag if buying liquid curry pastes or chilli sauces.

Bangkok 4-Day Itinerary Street Food Guide
Bangkok Street Food — Must Eats

The Indian Traveller's Bangkok Food Bible

Thai food is bold, aromatic, and completely different from what Indian restaurants serve back home. Try everything. Tell them "pet nit noi" (a little spicy) if you need to manage heat levels.

🍜 Pad Thai — ผัดไทย
Stir-fried rice noodles with egg, tofu, bean sprouts, peanuts, tamarind. Thailand's most famous dish. Order with shrimp (goong) or chicken (gai). Best from street stalls, not restaurants.
50–80 THB ≈ ₹120–₹192
🥗 Som Tum — ส้มตำ
Green papaya salad pounded in a mortar — chilli, lime, fish sauce, tomatoes, peanuts. Intensely sour, salty and spicy. Ask for "mai pet" (not spicy) if needed. Eaten with sticky rice.
40–80 THB ≈ ₹96–₹192 | Ask for "vegetarian" version
🥭 Mango Sticky Rice — ข้าวเหนียวมะม่วง
Fresh ripe mango over warm glutinous sticky rice, drizzled with sweet coconut cream and toasted sesame seeds. One of the world's great desserts. Best in mango season (April–June). Available year-round from street stalls.
60–100 THB ≈ ₹145–₹240
🍲 Tom Yum Soup — ต้มยำ
Hot and sour broth with lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime, chilli and shrimp. Tom Kha (coconut milk version) is milder and creamier. Order as a shared dish — comes in a small clay pot. Deeply fragrant.
80–150 THB ≈ ₹192–₹360
🍳 Khao Pad — ข้าวผัด
Thai fried rice — simpler than Pad Thai but incredibly satisfying. Made with jasmine rice, egg, garlic, fish sauce. Order with chicken, shrimp, or pork. Add a fried egg on top ("kai dao"). Everywhere, anytime, always good.
50–80 THB ≈ ₹120–₹192 | Great hangover food
🍢 Satay Skewers — สะเต๊ะ
Grilled chicken or pork on bamboo skewers, marinated in turmeric and coconut milk. Served with peanut sauce and cucumber pickle. Sold at every street corner from charcoal grills. Buy 5–10 at a time.
10–15 THB ≈ ₹24–₹36 per stick
🥥 Fresh Coconut — มะพร้าว
A whole young coconut, hacked open with a machete, served with a straw. Refreshing and hydrating in Bangkok's 32°C heat. Often served with a spoon to scoop out the soft coconut flesh. Found at every market and many 7-Elevens.
30–50 THB ≈ ₹72–₹120
🫓 Roti with Banana — โรตีกล้วย
A Thai-Muslim flatbread (roti) fried in butter on a griddle, stuffed with banana and drizzled with condensed milk and sugar. Thin, crispy, sweet, addictive. A Bangkok street food institution. Often sold late night near backpacker areas.
25–40 THB ≈ ₹60–₹96
Best Streets & Markets for Food
Yaowarat Road (Chinatown): Bangkok's best food street. Roast duck, dim sum, oyster omelettes, seafood. Go after 6pm when the street food stalls set up. Packed with locals and in-the-know visitors.
Sukhumvit Soi 38 Night Market: Convenient for Sukhumvit hotel guests. Pad Thai, grilled meats, papaya salad, mango sticky rice. Opens from 6pm daily. More accessible than Yaowarat for first-timers.
Or Tor Kor Market (near Chatuchak): Upscale fresh produce market — premium quality Thai fruits, prepared foods, and regional specialties. Cleaner and more comfortable than street stalls. Excellent for mangoes and tropical fruits to try before buying gifts.
7-Eleven: Your best friend in Bangkok. Exists on every corner (there are 14,000+ in Thailand). Cheap coffee (35 THB ≈ ₹84), ready meals, fresh sandwiches, cold drinks, snacks. Perfect for breakfast on the go or late-night hunger.

Bangkok 4-Day Itinerary Packing List & Pro Tips
What to Pack

Bangkok Packing List

Clothing

□Lightweight cotton / linen shirts (3–4) — Bangkok is 32°C and humid year-round
□Light trousers or long skirt (2) — for temple entry and air-conditioned malls
□Scarf or light shawl — mandatory for temple cover-ups, keep in bag at all times
□Shorts (2) — for casual days, markets and nightlife (not temples)
□Comfortable walking shoes — you'll walk 10–15km on temple days
□Flip flops — easy to remove at temples (shoes off required), beach/pool use
□One smart outfit — for rooftop bars (some have dress codes)

Health & Essentials

□Mosquito repellent (DEET-based) — mosquitoes active especially at dawn/dusk
□High SPF sunscreen (SPF 50+) — UV index extreme, reapply every 2 hours outdoors
□Power bank (20,000 mAh) — long days of Grab, maps and photos will drain your phone
□Small day bag / backpack — for temple days (don't carry full luggage)
□Diarrhoea tablets (Imodium) — street food is safe but stomachs adjust slowly
□Electrolyte sachets — sweating heavily in Bangkok heat causes dehydration
□Universal plug adapter — Thailand uses Type A/B plugs (same as India, mostly fine)
Pro Tips for Indian Travellers

Survive & Thrive in Bangkok

📱
Always Use Grab
Never negotiate with tuk-tuks or metered taxis. Grab is Thailand's Uber — fixed prices, AC, safe, transparent. Download before you fly. Set up payment with your Indian card (Visa/Mastercard works). Add a Thai number once you get your SIM. Tuk-tuks quote 5x the Grab price for tourists.
🏪
7-Eleven is Your HQ
Bangkok has more 7-Elevens per square km than almost anywhere on Earth. Cheap bottled water (10 THB ≈ ₹24), coffee, microwave meals, fresh sandwiches, ATM inside. They also sell sunscreen, mosquito repellent, and phone chargers. Open 24/7. Non-spicy snacks are great for sensitive stomachs.
🙏
Learn 3 Thai Phrases
Sawasdee (kha/krub): Hello (kha for women, krub for men — always add these). Khob Khun (kha/krub): Thank you. Aroy: Delicious — say this after eating street food and the vendor will love you. Thais are immensely proud of their food and culture — any effort with the language is met with huge smiles.
💵
Cash is King
Street food, markets, temples, tuk-tuks, river boats — all cash only. Exchange money at Superrich (yellow logo) — rates 3–5% better than banks. Avoid airport exchange counters. Carry small bills (20s and 50s) for boats and street food. ATMs charge 220 THB ≈ ₹530 per withdrawal — withdraw large amounts at once.
🛕
Temple Etiquette
Remove shoes before entering any temple building (even small neighbourhood ones). Never point feet toward a Buddha image — it's deeply disrespectful. Do not touch Buddha statues. Women should not hand anything directly to monks. Dress modestly — sarongs are available at major temples to borrow. Speak softly inside temple halls.
🌡
Beat the Heat
Bangkok is 32°C+ with 80% humidity from March–October. Best months: November–February (cooler, drier). Plan outdoor activities (temples, markets) before noon. Rest in malls or your hotel 12–3pm. Carry a small water bottle always — drink constantly. The BTS and MRT are gloriously air-conditioned — take the long route if needed to cool down.
India-specific tip: Thai restaurants near tourist areas often have Indian food on the menu — avoid it. The real Thai food is on the streets for a fraction of the price and 10x better. Most Thai curries use coconut milk base and are not extremely spicy — easier on Indian stomachs than you'd expect. Try everything at least once.
