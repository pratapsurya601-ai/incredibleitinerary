Day 1 — Forts
🏯 Amber Fort & Hill Forts
Amber Fort is Rajasthan's finest fort-palace — its Sheesh Mahal (Hall of Mirrors) interior, where thousands of glass fragments create a starlit effect by candlelight, is among the most beautiful interiors in India. Jaigarh Fort houses Asia's largest cannon on wheels. Nahargarh provides the city's best sunset panorama.
Day 2 — Old City
🏛️ City Palace & Walled City
City Palace (still partially inhabited by the royal family) and its extraordinary museum of royal weapons, textiles and palanquins. The astronomical observatory Jantar Mantar (UNESCO) with 19 instruments, including the world's largest stone sundial accurate to 2 seconds. Hawa Mahal's 953 windows and Johari Bazaar's gem traders.
Day 3 — Leisure
🌊 Jal Mahal & Chokhi Dhani
Jal Mahal — the five-storey palace rising from the middle of Man Sagar Lake, with four storeys submerged below water. Best photographed from the road at sunrise or sunset. Then Chokhi Dhani village resort in the evening — a recreated Rajasthani village with folk performances, puppet shows, camel rides and a traditional thali dinner.
Throughout
🍽️ Royal Rajasthani Food
Dal baati churma — wheat dumplings baked over wood fire, dipped in dal and ghee with churma (sweetened crushed wheat). Laal maas — fiery mutton curry in Mathania red chillies. Ghewar — crisp disc-shaped sweet in sugar syrup. Lassiwala on MI Road (the most famous lassi in Rajasthan). Rawat Mishtan Bhandar for kachori.

"Jaipur was painted pink in 1876 to welcome Prince Albert, and never changed back. It is the only city in India that chose its own colour — and chose perfectly."

Best Time to Visit: October to March. November and February are ideal — 18–28°C, clear skies, the pink sandstone glowing in winter light. December and January are cooler (8–20°C) but beautifully clear. The Elephant Festival during Holi (March) is spectacular. Avoid April to September — temperatures reach 45°C and the forts are brutal by midday.

Jaipur 3-Day Itinerary Day 1–2 — Forts & Old City
01
Day One · The Hill Forts
Amber Fort, Jaigarh & Nahargarh Sunset
Morning Amber Fort · Sheesh Mahal · Jaigarh Fort · Nahargarh for sunset panorama of Jaipur
💰 Amber: ₹550 · Jaigarh: ₹200 · Nahargarh: ₹200 · Jeep up to Amber: ₹300 return
8:00 AM
Amber Fort — The Sheesh Mahal & Ganesh Pole
Amber Fort (11km from Jaipur city) is best visited in the early morning before tour groups arrive. Take a jeep from the base (₹300 return shared, or ₹150 one way) rather than elephant — see the ethics note below. The Diwan-i-Aam, Diwan-i-Khas and Jai Mandir are magnificent, but the Sheesh Mahal (Hall of Mirrors) is the centrepiece: a room entirely clad in small convex mirrors set in plaster, so that a single candle is reflected thousands of times, filling the space with stars. The Ganesh Pole gateway is the most photographed entrance in Rajasthan. Allow 2–2.5 hours.
Entry: ₹550 Indians · ₹200 foreigners · Audio guide: ₹150 (recommended) · Open 8am–5:30pm
11:00 AM
Jaigarh Fort — Asia's Largest Cannon on Wheels
Jaigarh Fort sits on the ridge above Amber — walk up from Amber (15 minutes uphill) or take a shared jeep (₹50). The fort contains Jai Vana, the world's largest cannon on wheels, cast in 1720 — 6 metres long, never fired in war. The fort also has a stepwell, a weapons museum and one of the best views over the Amber valley. The royal treasury was reportedly discovered here in the 1970s during searches ordered by Indira Gandhi's government — its existence and contents remain a state secret to this day.
Entry: ₹200 (ticket also covers Nahargarh on the same day) · Jaigarh ticket is separate from Amber ticket
Sunset
Nahargarh Fort — Best Sunset Panorama in Jaipur
Nahargarh (Tiger Fort), on the Aravalli ridge northwest of the city, has Jaipur's finest sunset viewpoint. The view sweeps over the entire pink city, the lakes, and on clear days to the horizon. There are several rooftop cafes inside the fort walls (Padao Restaurant and Cafe Nahargarh) where you can drink chai while watching the sun go down. In the evenings, the city lights below compete with the orange sky above. Auto from city: ₹150–₹200 return. The road up is steep and narrow.
Entry: ₹200 (or included if you visited Jaigarh same day) · Arrive 45 mins before sunset · Café chai: ₹60
02
Day Two · Old City & Bazaars
City Palace, Jantar Mantar & Johari Bazaar
City Palace museum · Jantar Mantar · Hawa Mahal exterior · Johari Bazaar gems · Albert Hall Museum evening
💰 City Palace: ₹700 · Jantar Mantar: ₹200 · Hawa Mahal: ₹50 · Albert Hall: ₹150
9:00 AM
City Palace — Still a Royal Residence
The Jaipur royal family (the Kachwaha dynasty) still lives in part of City Palace. The museum section includes the Chandra Mahal, Mubarak Mahal, Diwan-i-Khas and the armoury. The most extraordinary display: two silver urns in the Diwan-i-Khas, each weighing 340kg — the largest silver objects in the world. Made by the Maharaja to carry Ganges water to England (he refused to drink non-Ganga water). The royal costumes gallery shows garments of absurd size — Maharaja Sawai Madho Singh I was reportedly 1.2m wide.
Entry: ₹700 Indians · Camera: ₹200 extra · Allow 2 hours · Audio guide available at entrance
11:30 AM
Jantar Mantar — 19 Instruments, World's Largest Sundial
Built by Maharaja Jai Singh II between 1727–1734, Jantar Mantar contains 19 fixed astronomical instruments used to track celestial bodies, predict eclipses and calculate the Hindu calendar. The Samrat Yantra — a 27-metre sundial — is accurate to 2 seconds and can track the sun's movement in real time (the shadow moves 1mm per second). UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2010. Hire a guide at the entrance (₹200–₹300) — without explanation, the instruments appear as abstract sculptures; with explanation, they become breathtaking acts of intellect.
Entry: ₹200 · Guide: ₹200–₹300 (essential) · Located next to City Palace · 45 minutes to 1 hour
12:30 PM
Hawa Mahal — The Palace of Winds Exterior
The Hawa Mahal's iconic facade of 953 small windows (jharokhas) was built in 1799 to allow royal women — who observed purdah and could not appear in public — to watch street festivals below. The facade is only one room deep. Entry gives access to a small museum and the upper floors (₹50), but the most celebrated view is of the exterior from the street. Cross to the tea stall on the opposite side of the road for the classic photograph. Best photographed in morning light (it faces east). The Old City bazaar around it is excellent for browsing.
Entry: ₹50 · Best photographed from outside · Morning light on facade is best · Closed on public holidays
Afternoon
Johari Bazaar — Gems, Bangles & Rajasthani Textiles
Jaipur is India's gem capital — the city processes 80% of the world's coloured gemstones. Johari Bazaar (Jewellers' Market) has family-run gem traders who have dealt here for generations. Genuine stones are sold by weight (carats), certified by hallmark. Avoid anyone who approaches you on the street offering "special prices" on gems for resale abroad — this is a nationwide scam. For textiles: Bapu Bazaar for Rajasthani block-print cotton and bandhani (tie-dye). Nehru Bazaar for bangles and lac jewellery (₹50–₹500/set).
Johari Bazaar: free to browse · Bangles: ₹50–₹500 · Block-print dupatta: ₹300–₹1,500 · Gems: get certified dealers only

Day Three · Leisure & Culture
Jal Mahal, Sisodia Rani Garden & Chokhi Dhani
Jal Mahal morning · Sisodia garden · Albert Hall Museum · Chokhi Dhani cultural village evening
💰 Chokhi Dhani: ₹800–₹1,200 · Albert Hall: ₹150 · Total day: ₹1,200–₹2,000
7:00 AM
Jal Mahal — The Water Palace at Sunrise
Jal Mahal (Water Palace) rises from the middle of Man Sagar Lake — five storeys tall, with four below the waterline. The palace itself is closed to visitors (it's under renovation and protection), but the view from the lakeside road is extraordinary, especially at golden hour. Migratory birds roost in the trees around the lake from October to March — in November it becomes a bird photographer's paradise with pelicans, painted storks and spoonbills. The best vantage point is from the road to Amber (NH11), north of the city, where the road runs alongside the lake. Free to view from the road.
Free to view from road · Boats available seasonally: ₹50–₹100 · Best at sunrise or just before sunset · No entry to palace
10:00 AM
Sisodia Rani Garden & Vidyadhar Garden
8km east of the city, Sisodia Rani Garden was built in 1728 by Maharaja Jai Singh II for his second wife. A sequence of terraced gardens with painted pavilions, fountains and murals depicting the Radha-Krishna story — one of the loveliest and quietest spots in Jaipur. Vidyadhar Garden (nearby) is a formal Mughal-style garden built for the city's original planner, Vidyadhar Bhattacharya. Both are uncrowded and beautifully maintained. Good picnic spot. Auto from city: ₹120 return.
Sisodia Rani Garden: ₹50 · Vidyadhar Garden: ₹50 · Auto return from city: ₹120 · Allow 1.5 hours combined
Afternoon
Albert Hall Museum — Rajasthan's Finest Collection
Built in 1887 in Indo-Saracenic style, Albert Hall Museum houses Rajasthan's most comprehensive collection of decorative arts: miniature paintings, weapons, textiles, Rajasthani pottery, natural history displays and the famous 4,000-year-old Egyptian mummy in the basement. The building itself — illuminated at night — is a magnificent example of Victorian-era architecture in India. Evenings (after 6pm) the museum is lit up beautifully. Best visited in the afternoon before heading to Chokhi Dhani.
Entry: ₹150 Indians · Camera: ₹50 · Open 9am–5pm (closed on national holidays) · Allow 1.5 hours
6:00 PM
Chokhi Dhani — Rajasthani Cultural Village
Chokhi Dhani (20km south of city) is a recreated Rajasthani village resort — kitsch in concept but genuinely enjoyable in execution. Folk musicians and dancers, puppet shows, camel rides, magic performers, fortune tellers, boat rides and a traditional Rajasthani thali dinner (dal baati churma, laal maas, ker sangri, bajra roti, multiple chutneys and sweets) served on a patal (leaf plate) while sitting cross-legged. Entry includes all performances and the dinner. Takes 3–4 hours to experience fully. Book through your hotel or at the gate.
Entry + dinner: ₹800–₹1,200/person · Taxi return from city: ₹400–₹500 · Opens 5pm · Bookings recommended in peak season

Jaipur 3-Day Itinerary Insider Tips & Packing List
Insider Tips — Read Before You Go

Know Before You Go

🐘
Elephant Rides at Amber — The Ethics
Elephant rides at Amber Fort are controversial. The animals are often kept in poor conditions and the work is physically damaging for them on the steep cobbled ascent. Wildlife groups recommend against it. The alternative — jeep rides (₹300 shared return) — reaches the fort just as quickly. If you choose the elephant, select operators who show welfare certificates. A third option: walk up (1.5km), which takes 20 minutes and gives better views of the fort walls.
💎
Blue Pottery — The Real Thing
Jaipur's famous blue pottery (made with a quartz paste, not clay) is sold widely but fake versions abound. The real thing: made by registered artisans in Sanganer (10km from city) or from Kripal Kumbh in Bani Park (the original family workshop). Look for the typical cobalt-blue and white patterns with Persian motifs. Plates ₹200–₹500, vases ₹500–₹3,000. Avoid roadside shops near forts — their "blue pottery" is usually Chinese-painted ceramic.
🍽️
Where to Eat — Real Jaipur Food
Lassiwala (MI Road) — the most famous lassi shop in Rajasthan. Opens at 8am, often sells out by noon. Thick, creamy, topped with malai — ₹80. Rawat Mishtan Bhandar (Station Road) for Jaipur kachori, pyaaz kachori and mirchi vada. Suvarna Mahal at Rambagh Palace for a splurge dal baati churma with live folk music (₹2,000+/person). LMB (Laxmi Misthan Bhandar) in Johari Bazaar for raj kachori and sweets since 1954.
🚗
Getting Around Jaipur
Jaipur has a new metro but it doesn't serve the major tourist sites well. Autos are the best option — negotiate a price before getting in (roughly ₹15/km, minimum ₹80). Many hotels offer bicycle rental (₹150–₹200/day) which is the best way to explore the Old City lanes. Taxis (booked via hotels or Uber) are best for the day trip to Amber (₹800–₹1,200 return including wait time). Auto drivers near Amber aggressively approach tourists — only use government-registered taxis from the fort parking area.
🎭
Jaipur Literature Festival (January)
The Jaipur Literature Festival (usually last week of January) is the world's largest free literary festival — 250+ authors from 40+ countries over 5 days at Diggi Palace. Attendance is free, registration online required. If your visit coincides, it's a remarkable experience. The city fills up during the festival — book accommodation 2–3 months in advance and expect higher prices.
🧵
Shopping — What & Where
Block-print fabric: Anokhi Museum of Hand Printing (Amber Road) has a fixed-price shop and shows the process. Rajasthani quilts (rezai): Bapu Bazaar, ₹800–₹3,000. Miniature paintings: look for artists who will do them in front of you on Hawa Mahal road. Lac bangles: any old city shop, ₹50–₹500/set. Avoid the "government export approved" shops near forts — they are commission traps and 50–100% overpriced compared to the bazaar.
🌡️
Seasons — Jaipur Weather Guide
October–November: post-monsoon, pleasant, the city freshly washed. December–January: cold at night (4–8°C), warm days (20–25°C), clear skies — pack a jacket for evenings and early mornings at Nahargarh. February–March: best weather, Holi in March transforms the city. April: already 38°C. May–June: 45°C+ — forts retain heat and are genuinely punishing after 9am. Monsoon (July–September): dramatic skies, occasional flooding, but the Amber moat fills and the countryside turns green.
🏛️
Jaipur City Palace — Guided vs Self Visit
The City Palace is significantly better with a guide — the historical context of the silver urns, the royal garments display and the architectural layers requires explanation. Guides at the gate charge ₹400–₹600 for 2 hours; government-licensed guides show ID. The premium ticket (₹2,500) includes a heritage walk, private museum section access and a welcome drink — worth it for history enthusiasts. The regular ticket (₹700) covers all the major sections adequately.
Packing for Jaipur
🧥 Warm layer for evenings — Nahargarh at sunset in December is cold. A light fleece or shawl is essential.
👟 Comfortable walking shoes — Amber Fort's cobbled ascent and the Old City bazaars involve hours on uneven surfaces.
🕌 Full-coverage clothing — Rajasthan is more conservative than Mumbai or Goa. Cover shoulders and knees in the old city.
🧴 SPF 50 sunscreen — the Rajasthani sun is powerful even in winter. Amber Fort's open courtyards have minimal shade.
💧 2L water bottle — rehydration is critical. Jaipur's dry heat causes dehydration fast even in winter months.
💵 Cash in mixed notes — bazaars, autos, auto guides and entry fee windows are all cash. Keep ₹3,000 in small notes.
📷 Camera card — Amber Fort photography surcharge is ₹200. Budget for it upfront.
🎒 Tote bag — for bazaar shopping. You will buy things you did not plan to buy. An extra folding tote prevents panic.
🦟 Insect repellent — evenings at Man Sagar Lake (Jal Mahal area) and Chokhi Dhani's outdoor area have mosquitoes.
One last thing: Jaipur rewards wandering. Between the planned stops, get lost in the old city lanes — discover the neighbourhood temple, the chai shop with a 50-year-old proprietor, the family selling block-print fabric from their living room. The famous sights are genuinely wonderful. But the city between them is extraordinary too.
