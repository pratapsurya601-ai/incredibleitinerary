Day 6
🌄 Nagarkot — Everest Panorama
Nagarkot village (2,100m, 30km from Kathmandu) is the closest point to Kathmandu from which you can see Mount Everest on a clear day. The sunrise panorama — Everest, Lhotse, Cho Oyu, Langtang range and dozens of Himalayan peaks — is one of Nepal's most spectacular views. Stay overnight for the sunrise experience.
Day 7 Option
🐘 Chitwan National Park
Nepal's premier wildlife destination — UNESCO World Heritage jungle at the base of the Himalayas. One-horned rhinoceroses, Bengal tigers, gharial crocodiles, sloth bears, over 500 bird species. Jeep safari and elephant observation platforms (note: ethical elephant interactions only — no elephant riding). Entry ₹2,500 + jeep rental ₹3,000.

"Nepal is not a place where you go to see things — it's a place where you go to become something. The mountains have a way of clarifying everything that matters." Every peak visible from Kathmandu is among the world's highest.

No Visa Required for Indian Citizens: Indian passport holders do NOT need a visa for Nepal — simply carry your Indian passport (or voter ID card, but passport is recommended). There is no immigration stamp fee, no prior application. Indians are treated as virtually domestic visitors. Indian Rupees are accepted throughout Nepal — 100 INR = approximately 160 NPR. Keep some NPR for remote areas. Indian SIM cards with international roaming work in Nepal; alternatively, a Nepali SIM at Tribhuvan Airport costs ₹300 with 3GB data.
Flights: Delhi/Mumbai/Kolkata to Kathmandu (KTM) — ₹8,000–₹22,000 return (IndiGo, Air India, SpiceJet). One of the cheapest international routes from India. The 1-hour flight from Delhi is shorter than many domestic Indian routes. Alternatively: overland crossing at Sunauli (UP border) — tourist bus from Varanasi or Gorakhpur, but the journey takes 8–10 hours. For most travellers, flying is the clear choice.

Kathmandu — Pashupatinath, Boudhanath & Durbar Squares

💰 Pashupatinath: free for Indians · Boudhanath: ₹250 (foreigners) · Bhaktapur: ₹1,200
Day 1
Pashupatinath Temple — Sacred Shiva Shrine on the Bagmati
Pashupatinath is one of the most sacred Shiva temples in the world and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The main temple complex is free for Indian and South Asian Hindus (the inner sanctum is restricted to Hindus only — non-Hindus view from across the Bagmati River). The ghats along the Bagmati are where Hindu cremations take place continuously — sacred, solemn, and open to respectful observers (maintain distance and silence, no photography of cremations). Sadhus (Hindu holy men) in saffron robes sit along the ghats. The evening aarti at Pashupatinath is smaller and more intimate than Varanasi but equally profound. Dress modestly, remove shoes in temple areas.
Entry for Indians: free · Non-Indians: ₹1,200 · Aarti: free · Dress modestly, no photography of cremations
Day 1 PM
Boudhanath Stupa — The Largest Stupa in the World
The Boudha stupa (Boudhanath or Bodhnath) is the largest stupa in the world — 36 metres high, with a base circumference of 100 metres. The famous painted eyes of the Buddha gaze out from the four sides of the tower. The stupa is the most important Tibetan Buddhist site outside Tibet — scores of monasteries surround it, and Tibetan monks in maroon robes circumambulate the stupa constantly at dawn and dusk. The evening circumambulation (pradakshina) with butter lamps lit and monks chanting is one of Nepal's most moving rituals. Entry: ₹250 for foreigners (Indians may be charged the foreigner rate — carry change). The surrounding market has excellent thangka paintings and singing bowls.
Entry: ₹250 · Best at dawn (5:30am) or dusk · Butter lamp offering: ₹50 · Thangka paintings: ₹800–₹15,000
Day 2 AM
Swayambhunath — The Monkey Temple at Sunrise
Swayambhunath (Monkey Temple) sits on a hilltop 2km west of Kathmandu — 365 steps to the top (one for each day of the year). Visit at sunrise (6am) for the golden light on the stupa, the city spread below, and the hundreds of macaque monkeys that live here (don't carry food visibly). The stupa is one of Nepal's oldest religious sites — estimated to be 2,500 years old, though the current structure dates to the 15th century. The hilltop has a cluster of temples, shrines, and vajras. Entry: ₹250 for foreigners. Indians may be admitted free as Hindus — ask at the gate politely.
Entry: ₹250 · Go at sunrise 6am for best light · Keep food hidden from macaques · 365 steps
Days 2–3
Three Durbar Squares — Medieval Nepal Preserved
The Kathmandu Valley has three historic Durbar Squares (royal palace squares) — Kathmandu, Patan, and Bhaktapur — each a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Bhaktapur is the most intact and the most beautiful: the 55-Window Palace, the Nyatapola pagoda (Nepal's tallest, 5 storeys), pottery square, and traditional Newari architecture largely unaffected by the 2015 earthquake. Entry to Bhaktapur: ₹1,200 (mandatory tourist fee collected at city entrance). Patan (Lalitpur) Durbar Square: ₹600, magnificent stone sculptures, the Krishna temple. Kathmandu Durbar Square: ₹600, Kumari Ghar (home of the Living Goddess — the Kumari may appear at the window).
Bhaktapur: ₹1,200 · Patan: ₹600 · Kathmandu Durbar: ₹600 · Combined valley day: ₹2,400

Day 4
Pokhara — Nepal's Most Beautiful City
Pokhara sits at 800m in a valley beside Phewa Lake with the Annapurna range directly behind — Machhapuchhre (Fishtail Mountain, 6,993m) is visible from the lakeside. Travel from Kathmandu: tourist bus (8 hours, ₹800) or domestic flight (25 minutes, ₹4,000 — highly recommended). The lakeside (Lakeside area) is Pokhara's tourist hub — guest houses, cafés, boat rental, and the starting point for trekking and paragliding. Rent a rowing boat on Phewa Lake (₹500/hour) and row to Tal Barahi temple on the island. The reflection of the snow peaks in the lake at dawn is extraordinarily beautiful.
Flight KTM–Pokhara: ₹4,000 · Tourist bus: ₹800 · Rowing boat: ₹500/hr · Lakeside guest house: ₹800–₹3,000
Day 5 4AM
Sarangkot Sunrise — The Annapurna Range Revealed
Sarangkot (1,600m) is a viewpoint 12km from Pokhara reachable by jeep (₹400, takes 30 minutes — the road is rough). Leave by 4:30am to reach the summit before dawn. As the sky brightens, the entire Annapurna range — Dhaulagiri (8,167m), Annapurna I (8,091m), Annapurna South, Machhapuchhre (Fishtail, 6,993m), Annapurna III, Annapurna IV — lights up gold and pink in sequence as the sun rises. This is the most accessible Himalayan sunrise panorama in the world — no trekking required. Many visitors say it's the most beautiful thing they've ever seen. In the afternoon, paraglide from Sarangkot down to the lakeside (landing on the beach at Phewa Lake).
Jeep to Sarangkot: ₹400 · Viewpoint: free · Paragliding from Sarangkot: ₹8,000–₹12,000
Day 5 PM
Paragliding Over the Himalayas — World Class Experience
Pokhara is consistently rated one of the top 5 paragliding destinations in the world — the thermal updrafts from the Himalayas create ideal conditions. Paragliding companies operate from Sarangkot (launch) to Phewa Lake (landing), flying over the lake with the Annapurna range ahead of you. Flight duration: 20–40 minutes. Tandem flights with an experienced pilot (₹8,000–₹12,000 depending on duration). Reputable companies: Blue Sky Paragliding, Sunrise Paragliding, High Ground Adventures. The GoPro video package (₹2,000 extra) is worth getting — you'll want to share this. Age limit: 5–80 years, weight limit typically 120kg.
Tandem paragliding: ₹8,000–₹12,000 · GoPro video: ₹2,000 extra · Book evening before or same morning

Day 6
Nagarkot — Where You Can See Everest from Your Hotel Window
Nagarkot village sits at 2,100m, 30km east of Kathmandu on a ridge with sweeping Himalayan views. On a clear day (October–November and March–April are clearest), the panorama includes Mount Everest (8,849m), Lhotse, Cho Oyu, Makalu, and dozens of other peaks across 150km of Himalayan horizon. Stay overnight at one of the ridge-top hotels (₹2,000–₹5,000/night for hotel with mountain view rooms). Watch the sunset over the Himalayas in the evening, then wake before dawn for the spectacular sunrise when the peaks glow pink and gold. The Hotel at the End of the Universe and Club Himalaya are popular choices.
Taxi from Kathmandu to Nagarkot: ₹1,500 · Hotel with mountain view: ₹2,000–₹5,000/night
Day 7 Option A
Chitwan National Park — Rhinos, Tigers & Jungle
Chitwan National Park is a 5-hour drive or 25-minute flight from Kathmandu/Pokhara. Nepal's premier wildlife reserve holds one-horned rhinoceroses (the park's reintroduction program has been a global conservation success), Bengal tigers, gharial and mugger crocodiles, Ganges river dolphins, Asian elephants, sloth bears, leopards and over 500 bird species. Jeep safari (half day: ₹3,000/jeep) and guided jungle walks are the best ways to see wildlife. The best time for wildlife: early morning safaris. Rhinos are commonly seen. Tigers are present but elusive. Entry fee: ₹2,500 (Nepalese charge; Indians and foreigners pay the same rate).
Park entry: ₹2,500 · Jeep safari (half day): ₹3,000/jeep (4 people) · Canoe safari: ₹800/person
Day 7 Option B
Final Kathmandu — Thamel Shopping & Departure
Thamel is Kathmandu's tourist hub — a grid of narrow lanes packed with trekking gear shops, handicraft stores, pashmina, Thangka paintings, singing bowls, yak wool products, and restaurants. Excellent buys: genuine cashmere and pashmina (₹1,000–₹5,000 — feel the softness, not all advertised pashmina is real), Thangka paintings (detailed Tibetan Buddhist paintings on cotton, ₹2,000–₹30,000), singing bowls (₹500–₹3,000), brass deity figurines, trekking equipment. The Garden of Dreams (₹200) near Thamel is a restored Edwardian neoclassical garden — peaceful contrast to Thamel chaos. Pashupatinath for a final visit before evening flight.
Thamel: free to browse · Garden of Dreams: ₹200 · Pashmina: ₹1,000–₹5,000 · Thangka: ₹2,000–₹30,000

Nepal is the most affordable international destination from India — a full dal bhat meal (Nepal's national dish: lentil soup, rice, vegetable curries, pickle) costs ₹300–₹500 and is filling and nutritious. Trekking gear in Thamel is 50–70% cheaper than in India or Europe.

🏔️
Best Season — October & March Are Magic
October and November (post-monsoon): crystal clear skies, perfect Himalayan views, moderate temperatures. March and April: spring flowers, slightly hazier skies but warmer. December–February: cold in Kathmandu (2–10°C), but clear mountain views — worth it. Monsoon (June–September): trekking difficult, Kathmandu valley green and lush, mountain views obscured. Avoid June–August for mountain photography.
💰
Indian Rupees Work in Nepal
100 Indian Rupees = approximately 160 Nepali Rupees — carry some INR and exchange at official money changers in Kathmandu (Thamel has many). Indian currency is accepted in most tourist shops and restaurants in Kathmandu and Pokhara. However, avoid ₹2,000 notes — they are often refused. Use ₹500 and smaller notes. ATMs in Kathmandu dispense NPR — most accept Indian debit cards.
🥾
Trekking — Even a Half Day Changes Everything
You don't need to do the famous 14-day Everest Base Camp or Annapurna Circuit treks to experience Nepal's mountains. The Annapurna Panorama Trek (3–5 days) starting from Pokhara offers extraordinary views with limited effort. The World Peace Stupa hike from Pokhara lakeside (2 hours return) gives Annapurna range views. Even a 1-hour walk above Nagarkot feels like the world's rooftop.
🙏
Hindu Temple Etiquette in Nepal
Remove shoes before entering any temple complex. Non-Hindus are restricted from entering the inner sanctum of Pashupatinath — respect this boundary. Circumambulate stupas and mani walls clockwise (keep them on your right). Never step over offerings. Photography inside temples may be restricted — ask first. Dress modestly: cover shoulders and knees at all religious sites.
Altitude Note: Kathmandu is at 1,400m and Pokhara at 800m — no serious altitude problems expected for most visitors. Nagarkot at 2,100m is still well within safe range. If you plan to trek above 3,000m (such as Poon Hill at 3,210m or Jomsom), acclimatise gradually, stay hydrated, and do not ascend more than 300–400m per day above 3,000m. Symptoms of altitude sickness: headache, nausea, dizziness — descend immediately if symptoms worsen.
👟 Comfortable walking shoes — Kathmandu's streets are uneven and dusty. Good grip important for temple steps.
🧥 Warm layer for mornings — Nagarkot and Sarangkot sunrises are cold (5–10°C Oct–Mar). Fleece essential.
💊 Altitude tablets (Diamox) — only if trekking above 3,000m. Consult your doctor before taking preventatively.
📷 Good camera with telephoto — Himalayan peaks are distant even from viewpoints. A 200mm zoom captures Everest well from Nagarkot.
💧 Water purification tablets — tap water in Nepal is not safe to drink. Boil, filter or use tablets. Buy bottled water throughout.
🦟 Mosquito repellent (Chitwan) — malaria risk in Chitwan area, especially at dusk. DEET-based repellent essential.
💰 Small denomination cash — ₹100 and ₹50 Indian notes, or NPR for temple entry, taxis and tips. Many small vendors don't take cards.
🎒 Daypack (20–30L) — for Sarangkot hike/jeep, Boudhanath circumambulation, trekking. Keep it light.
📋 Passport (not voter ID) — voter ID technically works for Indian citizens but passport is recommended. Always carry a photocopy too.
