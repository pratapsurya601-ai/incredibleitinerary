Day 1
🏛️ Colaba & Marine Drive
The historic heart of old Bombay. Gateway of India facing the Arabian Sea, the Taj Mahal Palace Hotel — a masterpiece of Indo-Saracenic architecture — Colaba Causeway market, and the legendary Marine Drive seafront promenade at sunset. South Mumbai at its most elegant.
Day 2
🏭 Dharavi & Bandra
Dharavi — Asia's most productive informal settlement, not a slum but an industrial township with a GDP of over $1 billion. Then Bandra: the city's coolest neighbourhood, home to Bollywood stars, sea-facing Carter Road, and Linking Road shopping.
Day 3
⛵ Elephanta Caves & Juhu
UNESCO World Heritage rock-cut caves on Elephanta Island, reached by ferry from Gateway of India. The 6th-century Trimurti sculpture — Shiva with three heads — is one of India's greatest surviving sculptures. Then Juhu Beach and Siddhivinayak Temple to close.
Throughout
🍽️ Street Food Paradise
Vada pav (Mumbai's burger, ₹15–25) is the lifeblood of this city. Pav bhaji at Chowpatty or Juhu (₹80–150). Bhel puri, sev puri, dahi puri at any Chaat stall. The city's best meals cost less than ₹100 and are eaten standing on the pavement.

"Mumbai doesn't sleep, it shifts. At 3am the fish market at Sassoon Dock is at full volume. At 5am the dabbawalas begin. This city never stops performing."

Best Time to Visit: November to February — cooler (20–30°C), minimal humidity, clear skies. March to May gets hot and muggy (32–38°C). Monsoon (June–September) transforms Mumbai dramatically — flooded streets, but the city's resilience is something to witness. Avoid monsoon for a first visit.

Mumbai 3-Day Itinerary Day 1–2 — Colaba, Dharavi & Bandra
01
Day One · South Mumbai
Gateway, Taj Hotel & Marine Drive Sunset
Colaba Causeway · Gateway of India · Taj Hotel exterior · CST Railway Station · Marine Drive evening walk
💰 Budget day: ₹400–₹700 · Mid-range: ₹1,500–₹3,000 (excl. accommodation)
9:00 AM
Gateway of India — The Arch That Saw the British Leave
Built in 1924 to commemorate King George V's visit, the Gateway of India became famous as the departure point for the last British regiment when India achieved independence in 1948. Stand here and absorb that history. The harbour view is excellent in the morning before the crowds build. The Taj Mahal Palace Hotel (1903) looms behind you — even the exterior is worth photographing. Walk the promenade along the harbour.
Entry: free · Best before 10am to avoid crowds · Boat touts here are aggressive — ignore firmly
10:30 AM
Colaba Causeway Market — Antiques, Jewellery & Quirky Finds
The street market running south from the Gateway is one of Mumbai's best browsing experiences. Silver jewellery, Rajasthani textiles brought to Mumbai's tourist market, secondhand books, old Bollywood posters, brass items and genuine antique furniture in the back shops. Bargaining is expected — start at 40% of the first asking price. Leopold Cafe (est. 1871, featured in Shantaram) is a few steps away for a coffee break, though it's touristy and pricey by Mumbai standards.
Browsing: free · Chai at local Irani cafe: ₹15 · Leopold Cafe coffee: ₹180
12:30 PM
Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Terminus (CST) — UNESCO Architecture
A 15-minute walk or short taxi from Colaba. Built in 1887 and a UNESCO World Heritage Site, CST is arguably the most spectacular railway station in the world — Victorian Gothic meeting Indian Baroque, gargoyles and peacocks carved in stone, stained glass and a working station of astonishing intensity. Over 3 million people pass through daily. Enter the main hall and look up. No photography of security areas.
Free to enter public areas · Arrive during commute hours (8-10am or 5-8pm) for full intensity
Evening
Marine Drive — The Queen's Necklace at Sunset
Take a taxi or walk 20 minutes north to Marine Drive — the 3.6km seafront promenade curving along the Arabian Sea. At sunset, it earns its name: the street lights form a necklace of gold along the curve of the bay. Sit on the concrete sea wall (called the Queen's Necklace wall) and watch the sun drop into the Arabian Sea. Chowpatty Beach is at the northern end — buy bhel puri (₹60–₹80) from the vendors and eat with your feet in the sand. A quintessential Mumbai evening.
Free · Bhel puri: ₹60–₹80 · Pav bhaji at Chowpatty: ₹120–₹180
02
Day Two · Real Mumbai
Dharavi, Dhobi Ghat & Bandra by the Sea
Morning Dharavi tour · Dhobi Ghat · Bandra Bandstand · Linking Road shopping · Carter Road sunset
💰 Dharavi tour: ₹800 · Transport: ₹200 by local train · Bandra dining: ₹400–₹2,000
9:00 AM
Dharavi Walking Tour — India's Most Misunderstood Neighbourhood
Dharavi is not what films portray. It is a functioning industrial settlement producing leather goods, recycled plastics, textiles and pottery that are exported worldwide. A guided walking tour (Reality Tours is the most ethical operator — 80% of profits go back to the community) takes you through the recycling district, pottery colony, leather workshops and into the residential areas. No photography inside the residential zones. Tours run 9am and 2pm daily, 2–3 hours. Book at realitytoursmumbai.com.
Reality Tours: ₹800/person · Departs from Mahim station · 2–3 hours · Book in advance
12:00 PM
Dhobi Ghat — Open-Air Laundry, A Working Wonder
Near Mahalaxmi station (one stop north of Mahim by local train, ₹10). The world's largest open-air laundry, where thousands of dhobis (washermen) have washed Mumbai's laundry for 140 years. View from the road above for free — the sight of 800+ concrete wash pens, lines of drying clothes and men beating laundry on stone is unlike anything else. It is a working facility, not a tourist attraction — respect the workers and don't descend into the ghat without permission.
Free to view from the road bridge · Local train Mahim to Mahalaxmi: ₹10 · 30-minute visit
2:00 PM
Bandra — Mumbai's Most Alive Neighbourhood
Take a train or taxi to Bandra West. Bandstand Promenade is a sea-facing walkway with Mumbai's most famous landmark: the Shahrukh Khan bungalow Mannat (visible from the road, you can't enter). The nearby Mount Mary Church (1640s, rebuilt) is architecturally beautiful and spiritually significant. Then walk to Linking Road for Mumbai's best value shopping — branded outlets, street fashion, shoes, bags. Bargain hard on the street stalls.
Bandstand: free · Church: free · Linking Road: ₹200–₹2,000 for shopping
Sunset
Carter Road — Bandra's Sunset Boulevard
Carter Road is where Bandra's creative crowd gathers at golden hour — the walkway faces west and the sunset over the Arabian Sea here is spectacular. Street food vendors sell corn on the cob, pav bhaji and fresh coconut water. Several excellent cafes and restaurants line the road — Bastian for seafood (upscale, ₹1,500/person), or Social for craft beer and pub food (₹600–₹800). The vibe is lively, cosmopolitan and the best face of new Mumbai.
Walking: free · Coconut water: ₹40 · Dinner: ₹400–₹2,000 depending on choice

03
Day Three · Island & Temples
Elephanta Caves, Juhu & Siddhivinayak
9am ferry from Gateway · UNESCO caves · Juhu Beach · Siddhivinayak Temple or Haji Ali Dargah · Departure
💰 Ferry + caves: ₹600–₹800 · Juhu: ₹200 food · Total day: ₹1,000–₹2,000
9:00 AM
Ferry to Elephanta Island — UNESCO World Heritage Caves
Ferries depart from the Gateway of India jetty hourly from 9am (last return around 5:30pm). The 1-hour ride across Mumbai Harbour is itself a pleasure — the city skyline behind you, fishermen's boats, the occasional tanker. Buy tickets at the jetty counters. Once on the island, a miniature toy train (₹20) takes you to the base of the steps, or walk the 10-minute path through souvenir stalls. 120 steps up to the caves — bring water.
Ferry: ₹200 return (approx) · Caves entry: ₹40 Indians · Toy train: ₹20 · Total: ~₹260
10:30 AM
The Main Cave — Trimurti Sadashiva, India's Greatest Sculpture
The main Elephanta Cave (Cave 1) was carved in the 6th–7th century AD. The centrepiece is the Trimurti Sadashiva — a 6-metre-high triple-headed bust of Shiva showing three aspects: creator, preserver and destroyer. It is one of the finest examples of Gupta-Chalukya sculpture in existence. The cave also contains sculptural panels of Shiva's cosmic dance (Nataraja), Ardhanarishvara (half-male half-female Shiva), and the wedding of Shiva and Parvati. Allow 1.5–2 hours to do it justice.
Guide available at entrance: ₹400 for 2 hours (recommended, the sculptures need context)
2:00 PM
Juhu Beach — Chaotic, Joyful, Utterly Mumbai
After returning by ferry, take a taxi north to Juhu (30–40 minutes, ₹300–400). Juhu Beach is not a swimming beach — it's a social beach where Mumbai's northern suburbs come to breathe. The street food here is famous: pav bhaji (₹120), bhel puri (₹60), grilled corn, fresh juice. It's crowded, noisy and completely alive. Several Bollywood stars live near Juhu — Amitabh Bachchan's residence Jalsa is 5 minutes away (not open, just the gate).
Beach: free · Pav bhaji: ₹120 · Bhel puri: ₹60 · Fresh coconut: ₹40
5:00 PM
Siddhivinayak Temple or Haji Ali Dargah — Your Choice
Two iconic Mumbai spiritual sites, both near Juhu by taxi. Siddhivinayak Temple (Prabhadevi) is the most visited Ganesh temple in India — Tuesday darshans see extraordinary queues (avoid Tuesdays), other days are manageable. Entry is free. Haji Ali Dargah, the 15th-century tomb of Muslim saint Haji Ali Shah Bukhari, sits on a tiny islet 500m offshore, connected by a narrow causeway. Visit at high tide when the causeway is partially submerged for a dramatic approach. Both are non-negotiable Mumbai experiences.
Both free · Siddhivinayak: avoid Tuesdays · Haji Ali: accessible except during very high tide

Mumbai 3-Day Itinerary Insider Tips & Packing List
Insider Tips — Read Before You Go

Surviving & Thriving in Maximum City

🚇
Mumbai Local Train — The Lifeline
The local train network covers the city for ₹10–₹30 per journey. Three main lines: Western (Churchgate to Virar), Central (CST to Kasara) and Harbour (CST to Panvel). For tourists: buy a one-day tourist pass (₹85 for Western line). Ladies compartment is strictly women-only — non-compliance draws immediate and justified anger. Avoid peak hours (8–10am, 6–9pm) unless you want the full Mumbai experience.
🍟
Must-Eat Street Food Guide
Vada pav (potato fritter in a bun with chutneys, ₹15–25) — try Ashok Vada Pav at Dadar station, considered the original. Pav bhaji at Sardar Refreshments (Tardeo). Bhel puri: any beachside stall. Misal pav at Aaswad (Dadar) — the spiciest version imaginable. Bombay sandwich (green chutney, vegetables, butter) for ₹50 at any local stall — an underrated gem.
🌧️
Monsoon Warning (June–September)
Mumbai's monsoon is spectacular but disruptive. The city receives 2,400mm of rain annually — almost all between June and September. Flooding on 26 July 2005 brought 944mm in 24 hours. Elephanta ferries are suspended during heavy rain. Roads flood. Local trains often delay. If visiting in monsoon: keep your itinerary flexible, carry waterproof footwear (your shoes will be destroyed), and embrace the chaos. The city's spirit in monsoon is genuinely impressive.
🚗
Auto vs Uber — When to Use Each
Uber/Ola are priced fairly and app-metered — use for medium distances (₹80–₹300). Kaali-peeli (black and yellow taxis) use meters that run on an outdated card system — ask the driver to show you the conversion card. Autos (three-wheelers) are restricted from South Mumbai below Mahim — usable in Bandra, Andheri, Dharavi areas. For long distances, Uber is most reliable and transparent.
🏨
Where to Stay
Colaba: best location for historic sightseeing, budget guesthouses available, touristy but convenient. Bandra West: best neighbourhood experience, good food scene, needs more taxis. Andheri: near airport, cheaper, but far from most sights. Avoid staying in Navi Mumbai or Thane for a tourist trip — too far from everything worth seeing.
🎬
Bollywood: Film City Tours
Film City in Goregaon (Western line) offers studio tours — ₹1,500–₹3,000, book online. You may see sets being filmed but not actual shoots (schedules are not announced). The tour includes backlots, sets of famous serials and the film archive. For star spotting: Juhu and Bandra are where stars actually live. Siddhivinayak Temple on Tuesdays sees Bollywood celebrities arrive for darshan.
💧
Water & Food Safety
Mumbai's tap water is treated but carry bottled water for drinking. Street food from high-turnover stalls is generally safe — the vada pav vendor who sells 500 units before noon has fresher oil than the one with nobody in line. Avoid cut fruit from street stalls and pre-made juices from suspect stalls. Stomach issues are common in the first 2 days — a probiotic supplement beforehand helps.
🕌
Haji Ali — Timing is Critical
The causeway to Haji Ali Dargah is submerged during high tide — check tide times before visiting (search "Mumbai tide table"). The approach during high tide with water on both sides is dramatic. During very high tides access is cut off entirely. Visiting at sunset when the dargah is lit is the most atmospheric experience. Dress modestly: covered shoulders and long trousers/skirt for both men and women.
Packing for Mumbai
👟 Comfortable walking shoes — Mumbai involves enormous amounts of walking on uneven surfaces. Open sandals are fine for evenings.
🕌 Scarf or dupatta — required for temple visits. A light cotton scarf doubles as sun protection on Marine Drive.
🌧️ Compact umbrella — even in winter, brief showers are possible. In monsoon, a full raincoat is essential.
💊 ORS and antacid — street food adventures sometimes have consequences. Pack both.
🔋 Power bank — navigating Mumbai's neighbourhoods drains your phone battery. A 10,000mAh bank lasts the day.
💵 Cash in small notes — vada pav vendors don't take Paytm. Keep ₹100–₹500 in small notes for street food and autos.
📱 Uber app installed — with account set up before arrival. Saves time negotiating in a city where everyone is in a hurry.
🧴 Sunscreen SPF 50+ — Marine Drive, Juhu and Elephanta have zero shade. The Arabian Sea light is merciless.
👜 Small crossbody bag — pick pocketing exists in crowds. Keep passport and valuables in your hotel safe.
The honest truth about Mumbai: It is overwhelming, crowded, loud and relentless. It is also creative, generous, cosmopolitan and unlike anywhere else in India. The city rewards those who push through the initial sensory overload. By Day 2 you will begin to understand its rhythms. By Day 3 you will not want to leave.
