Cities & Highlights
Tokyo
Days 1–4

World's largest city and Japan's electric beating heart. Shibuya crossing, Harajuku street fashion, Shinjuku nightlife, Akihabara anime district, Asakusa temple, Tsukiji seafood, and the mind-bending TeamLab digital art.

Nikko / Kamakura
Day 3 or 4 Option

Nikko (2hrs): UNESCO shrine complex — the most ornate shrines in Japan. Tosho-gu is overwhelming in its gilded detail. Or Kamakura (1hr): Great Buddha + coastal temple town. Choose one.

Kyoto
Days 5–7

Japan's cultural and spiritual capital. 1,600 temples in a city. Fushimi Inari's 10,000 torii gates, Arashiyama bamboo grove, Gion geisha district, Nishiki Market. Every corner is a photograph.

Nara
Day 6 Half-Day

Sacred deer roam freely through the park, bowing for crackers. Todai-ji temple houses the world's largest bronze Buddha. 45 min from Kyoto — one of Japan's most magical detours.

Osaka
Days 8–9

Japan's kitchen and its most charismatic city. Dotonbori street food paradise, Osaka Castle, Kuromon Market, retro Shinsekai. More relaxed than Tokyo, more unfiltered than Kyoto. Wildly fun nightlife.

Hiroshima + Miyajima
Day 9 Day Trip

Peace Memorial Park and the A-Bomb Dome — emotional, necessary, unforgettable. Then ferry to Miyajima Island for the floating torii gate of Itsukushima Shrine. Deer here too. All covered by JR Pass.

Before You Fly

Pre-trip essentials

🛂
Japan Visa (Required)

Indians require a tourist visa — no visa on arrival. Apply at VFS Japan in Mumbai, Delhi, Chennai, Kolkata, or Bengaluru. Takes 5–7 working days. Submit bank statement (3 months), hotel bookings, detailed itinerary, return flight ticket.

Cost: ₹1,700 · Apply 3–4 weeks before travel

🚄
JR Pass — Buy in India

The Japan Rail Pass covers all Shinkansen (bullet train) travel + most local JR trains. 7-day ¥50,000 (₹27,500). 14-day ¥80,000. BUY BEFORE FLYING — it's significantly cheaper in India. Cannot be purchased after landing. Purchase from IRCTC Japan or authorised agents.

Covers: Tokyo ↔ Kyoto ↔ Osaka ↔ Hiroshima + Narita Express

🪙
IC Card: Suica or Pasmo

Rechargeable card for all local trains, buses, and metro across Japan. ¥500 deposit + load as needed. Also works at convenience stores, vending machines, and some restaurants. Get one at Narita/Haneda airport or major JR stations.

Load ¥3,000–5,000 to start · Refund deposit on departure

💴
Cash — Japan is Cash-First

Many restaurants, temples, and smaller shops are still cash-only. Do not rely on cards. 7-Eleven ATMs and Japan Post ATMs accept international Mastercard/Visa and give the best exchange rates. Withdraw ¥30,000–50,000 at once to minimise fees.

¥10,000 = ₹5,500 · Always carry ¥5,000–10,000 minimum

📶
SIM Card / Pocket WiFi

Buy at airport arrivals — IIJmio or B-Mobile SIM for ₹1,500 (15 days unlimited data). Alternative: Pocket WiFi rental from airport desk, ₹1,000/day, return at departure. Google Maps + Translate offline essential. Download Japanese for camera translate.

Also download: Hyperdia app for train navigation

🌦️
Weather + Timing Notes

Cherry blossom (sakura): late March to mid-April — magical but pack hotels 6–8 months ahead. Autumn foliage: mid-November — equally stunning, cooler. Avoid Golden Week (late April–early May) — everything is packed and expensive. Tenki.jp for accurate Japan forecasts.

Shoulder seasons = perfect weather + manageable crowds

Day 1 · Tokyo

Arrive Tokyo — Shinjuku First Night

Arrival Land at Narita (NRT) or Haneda (HND). Narita Express (N'EX) to Shinjuku station: ¥3,070, 1hr 30min — use your JR Pass here for the first time. Haneda is closer; JR train or monorail ¥500, 30 min.
Check-In Stay in Shinjuku or Shibuya. Budget hostels ¥2,500–4,000 (₹1,375–2,200). Business hotels ¥8,000–15,000. Mid-range recommendation: Shinjuku Granbell Hotel, or Super Hotel Shinjuku. Luxury: Park Hyatt Tokyo (Lost in Translation hotel) ¥30,000+.
Afternoon Shinjuku Gyoen Garden ¥500 (₹275) — 58 hectares of formal Japanese, French, and English gardens. Perfect for gentle jet-lag recovery. Spectacular during cherry blossom and autumn seasons.
Evening Omoide Yokocho ("Memory Lane") — a narrow alley crammed with tiny yakitori stalls, smoke, and salarymen since 1946. One of Tokyo's most atmospheric spots. Skewers ¥800–1,500. Atmospheric and slightly chaotic.
Night Shinjuku Kabukicho — Tokyo's famous entertainment district. Walk, observe, absorb. The Godzilla head on the Toho Cinemas building at night. Massive arcades, neon, bars. Safe to walk alone at any hour.
Day 2 · Tokyo

Shibuya + Harajuku + Meiji Shrine

8:00 AM Meiji Shrine (free, opens 6am) — a vast forested Shinto shrine dedicated to Emperor Meiji, right in the middle of Tokyo. The walk through the towering torii gates and cedar forest is genuinely meditative. 30 min walk from Harajuku station.
10:00 AM Harajuku — Takeshita Street — Tokyo's youth fashion epicentre. Rainbow crepes ¥500, vintage clothing, absurdly creative outfits. Walk Omotesando afterward for the luxury end of the spectrum (LVMH's Omotesando Hills is architecturally stunning).
12:00 PM Shibuya Crossing — the world's busiest pedestrian intersection. Up to 3,000 people cross at once from all directions. Cross it. Do it again. Best viewed from the second-floor Starbucks or from the Scramble Square rooftop. Free.
2:00 PM Shibuya Sky observation deck ¥2,000 — 360° open-air view from the top of Shibuya Scramble Square (46th floor). One of Tokyo's best city views. Book online 2–3 days ahead — sells out on clear days.
4:00 PM Daikanyama / Nakameguro canal walk — Tokyo's chicest neighbourhood. Daikanyama T-Site bookshop is an architectural and cultural experience. Nakameguro canal lined with cherry trees (magical in spring) and boutique cafes.
Evening Dinner: Ichiran Ramen ¥900 — solo booth ramen, private wooden partitions, the most focused dining experience possible. Or Midori Sushi Shibuya — conveyor belt sushi ¥100–300/plate, always fresh queue. Both are legendary.

Day 3 · Tokyo

Asakusa + Akihabara + Skytree + TeamLab

8:00 AM Senso-ji Temple, Asakusa (free, opens 6am) — Tokyo's oldest temple (645 AD), framed by the iconic Kaminarimon (Thunder Gate) with its enormous red lantern. Nakamise shopping street leading to the temple sells traditional Japanese souvenirs — ningyo dolls, fans, sembei rice crackers. Arrive before 9am to avoid coach tour crowds.
10:00 AM Asakusa area exploration — Nakamise shopping street for traditional crafts. The Hoppy Street nearby for daytime yakitori (unusual but locals do it). Azuma Bridge for classic Skytree + Senso-ji skyline photograph.
11:00 AM Tokyo Skytree ¥2,100 (₹1,155) — at 634m, the tallest structure in Japan and second tallest in the world. Views to Mt Fuji on clear days (winter mornings are clearest). Skip if it's overcast — waste of money. The shopping mall complex at the base is also excellent.
2:00 PM Akihabara Electric Town — seven-story electronics stores, anime figurines, manga, vintage video games, and the surreal world of maid cafes (¥500–1,000 for a coffee with theatrical service). Wild and fascinating even if you have zero interest in anime. Yodobashi Camera for electronics.
6:00 PM Tsukiji Outer Market for dinner — the inner tuna auction market moved to Toyosu, but the outer market remains active and excellent. Stalls serving fresh sashimi sets ¥1,500–3,000, grilled scallops ¥300, tamagoyaki (egg omelette on stick) ¥300. Come hungry.
8:00 PM TeamLab Planets, Toyosu ¥3,200 — the world's most spectacular immersive digital art installation. Walk through rooms of infinite mirrors, wade through a water room, lie on the floor under a galaxy ceiling. Book online weeks in advance — this sells out constantly. One of the best experiences in Japan.
Day 4 · Day Trip from Tokyo

Nikko or Kamakura — Choose Your Adventure

Option A — Nikko (Recommended for architecture lovers)
Travel JR Shinkansen + local train from Shinjuku to Nikko, approx 2 hours. JR Pass covers most of the journey. Leave by 8:30am to make the most of the day.
All Day Tosho-gu Shrine Complex ¥1,300 — UNESCO World Heritage Site and the most ornate, gilded, elaborate shrine complex in Japan. Built to enshrine Tokugawa Ieyasu, the shogun who unified Japan. Every surface is carved and lacquered. The famous three wise monkeys carving is here. Allow 3 hours minimum to walk the full complex.
Nearby Shinkyo Bridge — sacred red bridge over the Daiya River, one of Japan's three finest bridges. Rinno-ji Temple and surrounding cedar forests. Stunning in autumn foliage (mid-October to mid-November) — one of Japan's best autumn spots.
Option B — Kamakura (Recommended for coastal/relaxed feel)
Travel JR Yokosuka Line from Tokyo Station or Shinjuku, approx 1 hour. JR Pass covers this. Arrive Kamakura Station.
Morning Kotoku-in Temple — Great Buddha (Daibutsu) ¥300 — a 13.35m tall bronze Buddha seated outdoors since 1252. Serene, ancient, extraordinary. You can enter the hollow statue for an additional ¥20.
Afternoon Hase-dera Temple ¥400 — ocean views, cave of Benzaiten goddess, thousands of small Jizo statues. Then Tsurugaoka Hachimangu shrine — Kamakura's most important shrine. Coastal town vibe throughout — good seafood lunch ¥1,500–2,500.
Evening — Pack for Kyoto: Whichever day trip you choose, return to Tokyo by 6–7pm. Pack bags tonight. Tomorrow you take the Shinkansen to Kyoto (2hrs 20min). Lay out your JR Pass — it starts earning its keep tomorrow.

Days 5–7 · Kyoto

The Cultural Heart of Japan

Former imperial capital for over 1,000 years. 1,600 Buddhist temples, 400 Shinto shrines, 17 UNESCO World Heritage Sites. Every street in Kyoto has a story. Three days is barely enough — but it's a beginning.

Day 5 · Kyoto — Arrival Day

Fushimi Inari + Gion Evening

Morning Shinkansen: Tokyo → Kyoto 2hrs 20min (JR Pass covers this fully). Nozomi trains are fastest but not covered — take Hikari or Sakura. Depart Tokyo by 9am to arrive before noon. Sit on the right side for Mt. Fuji views (clear mornings, first 30 min).
Check-In Stay in Fushimiku, Gion, or Higashiyama area. A traditional machiya (townhouse guesthouse) is worth the premium — ¥12,000–20,000/night for the experience. Recommendations: Nishiyama Ryokan, Gion Nanba, or the more budget-friendly K's House Kyoto.
2:00 PM Fushimi Inari Shrine (free, open 24hrs) — 10,000 vermillion torii gates winding up Mount Inari for 4km. Walk to the first summit (Yotsutsuji intersection, 45 min) for city views and manageable crowds. Full circuit to the top: 2.5 hours. The deeper you go, the fewer tourists. Go in the afternoon, stay for dusk light through the gates.
Evening Gion district walk — Kyoto's preserved geisha neighbourhood. Hanamikoji Street is the most atmospheric at dusk: latticed wooden teahouses, paper lanterns, occasional kimono-clad figures. Do not photograph geisha (maiko) without permission — it's genuinely disrespectful and they move fast.
Dinner Nishiki Market for evening browsing (closes 6pm for most stalls). Dinner at Omen Restaurant ¥1,500 — simple, beautiful udon and tofu cuisine in a historic wooden space near Nanzen-ji. Or Gion side streets for kaiseki-lite set meals ¥2,000–3,500.
Day 6 · Kyoto + Nara

Arashiyama Bamboo + Sacred Deer of Nara

7:00 AM Arashiyama Bamboo Grove (free) — arrive before 8am for a near-crowd-free experience in one of the most otherworldly places on earth. 10 minutes of towering green bamboo stalks, light filtering through, absolute quiet. By 10am it's thick with tourists. Go early or miss the point entirely.
8:30 AM Tenryu-ji Temple ¥500 — UNESCO garden included. The stroll garden with a pond reflecting the Arashiyama hills is one of Kyoto's finest views. Then Jojakko-ji Temple ¥500 (five-minute walk) — a hidden gem of mossy stone steps, rare tree species, and a bamboo grove almost as striking as the famous one.
Optional Rickshaw ride through Arashiyama bamboo with a jinrikisha — ¥5,000–8,000 for 2 persons (30 min). Drivers speak some English and give commentary. Extremely Japanese experience.
11:00 AM JR train to Nara — 45 minutes from Kyoto Station (JR Nara Line, JR Pass covers). Nara Park: 1,200 freely roaming sacred Sika deer. Buy shika senbei (deer crackers) ¥200 and prepare to be surrounded. The deer genuinely bow for crackers.
1:00 PM Todai-ji Temple ¥600 — the world's largest wooden building (57m tall), housing a 15m bronze Buddha (Daibutsu) cast in 752 AD. The scale is humbling. The narrow wooden pillar with a hole at the base — same size as Buddha's nostril — is said to grant enlightenment if you squeeze through. Children and thin adults only.
3:00 PM Stroll Kasuga Taisha Shrine (free exterior) — ancient stone lanterns lining forested paths. Late afternoon light through the trees is exceptional. Then JR train back to Kyoto by 5pm. Dinner at your Kyoto accommodation area.
Day 7 · Kyoto — Final Day

Golden Pavilion + Philosopher's Path + Pontocho

9:00 AM Kinkaku-ji — Golden Pavilion ¥500 — Japan's most photographed structure. A Zen temple covered in gold leaf, reflected in a mirror pond. Arrive before 9:30am to beat the busiest crowds. The garden circuit takes 30–45 minutes. Do not expect silence — but the image is as transcendent as advertised.
11:00 AM Ryoan-ji Temple ¥600 — Japan's most famous rock garden: 15 stones arranged in raked white gravel, none ever fully visible at once. Deliberately enigmatic. Built 1499. Sit on the wooden platform and look for as long as you need. The large stroll pond garden surrounding it is also beautiful.
1:00 PM Philosopher's Path (Tetsugaku-no-Michi) — a 2km canal-side stone path named after philosopher Nishida Kitaro who walked it daily. Lined with 450 cherry trees (a tunnel of blossom in spring). Walk north to south, stopping at small shrines, cafes, and tofu shops along the way. Free.
2:30 PM Nanzen-ji Temple — free exterior, ¥600 for hojo garden. The enormous sanmon gate (1628) is one of Japan's most imposing entrances. The aqueduct running through the grounds is a surprising 19th-century addition. Walk up to the Nanzen-in sub-temple pond garden for solitude.
Evening Pontocho Alley — a narrow 500m lantern-lit alley of restaurants, sake bars, and jazz bars running parallel to the Kamo River. One of Kyoto's most atmospheric streets. Budget ¥2,000–4,000 for a full dinner. Reserve at better spots. Try tofu kaiseki or river-view dining on the raised platforms (kawayuka) in summer.

Days 8–10 · Osaka & Beyond

Japan's Kitchen & Its Most Sobering Day

Osaka is a relief after Kyoto's temple intensity — louder, funnier, less formal, obsessively food-focused. Then Day 9 takes you to Hiroshima and Miyajima for Japan's most emotionally complete experience.

Day 8 · Osaka

Osaka Castle + Dotonbori Street Food + Shinsekai

Morning Shinkansen or local train: Kyoto → Osaka — 15 min by Shinkansen (JR Pass), 30 min by local JR line (also covered). Check into Namba or Shinsaibashi area. Recommendation: Cross Hotel Osaka, or budget-friendly Dormy Inn Namba ¥7,000–9,000.
10:00 AM Osaka Castle ¥600 — built 1583 by Toyotomi Hideyoshi, who unified Japan. Eight-storey reconstruction is visually magnificent even if the interior is a museum. Osaka-jo Park surrounds it — free to explore, exceptional cherry blossom spot in spring. Allow 1.5 hours.
1:00 PM Dotonbori — the beating, neon heart of Osaka. The canal-side street of food stalls and restaurants is Japan's most concentrated street food experience. Must eat: Takoyaki (octopus balls, ¥500 for 6), Okonomiyaki (savoury pancake, ¥900), Kushikatsu at Daruma (deep-fried skewers, ¥1,500). The iconic Glico running man billboard is here.
4:00 PM Shinsekai neighbourhood — retro 1950s Osaka that feels like a film set. Cheap kushikatsu bars, blowfish restaurants, the Tsutenkaku Tower neighbourhood, old-school pachinko parlours. Locals and tourists in equal measure. Inexpensive and character-rich. Kushikatsu for ¥800–1,200.
Night Dotonbori at night — return for the neon in full effect. The reflection of signs in the canal is spectacular. Bar-hop along the canal or head to Namba area izakayas (Japanese pub restaurants, ¥2,000–3,000 for drinks + food). Osaka nightlife is the most approachable in Japan.
Day 9 · Day Trip — 3hrs Each Way

Hiroshima Peace Park + Miyajima Island

7:00 AM Shinkansen: Osaka → Hiroshima 1hr 20min (JR Pass covers fully). Take the first or second Nozomi/Sakura of the day from Shin-Osaka station. Arrive Hiroshima by 8:30–9am for maximum time.
9:00 AM Peace Memorial Park — the park stands at the hypocentre of the August 6, 1945 atomic bomb. The A-Bomb Dome (Genbaku Dome) — the only structure left standing near the blast — is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site and the most arresting piece of preserved history you will see. Free to approach.
10:00 AM Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum ¥200 — one of the most important museums in the world. Personal effects, photographs, testimonies of survivors. Allow 1.5–2 hours minimum. It will be difficult. It is necessary. The museum is extremely well curated in English.
1:00 PM Ferry to Miyajima Island ¥180 (JR Pass covers the Miyajima Ferry fully). 10-minute crossing. Itsukushima Shrine with its famous torii gate appearing to float in the sea at high tide — one of Japan's "three views" and the country's most iconic photograph. Low tide: walk out to the gate through the sand.
2:00 PM Explore Miyajima — freely roaming deer (smaller than Nara's), the five-storey pagoda, hiking trail up Mt Misen (cable car ¥1,800 return) for views across the Seto Inland Sea. Lunch: Hiroshima-style oysters grilled on the shell ¥500 each — the island is famous for them. Fresh anago (conger eel) over rice ¥1,800.
5:30 PM Ferry back to Hiroshima, Shinkansen back to Osaka. Arrive Osaka by 8–9pm. Light dinner: convenience store onigiri and beer counts tonight — it's been a full day. Or a quick Dotonbori bowl of ramen ¥900.
Day 10 · Osaka — Final Morning

Kuromon Market + Fly from Kansai (KIX)

8:00 AM Kuromon Ichiba Market — "Osaka's Kitchen." A 600m covered market open since 1822, with 170 stalls of fresh seafood, Wagyu beef, produce, and prepared food. Breakfast here: grilled scallops ¥300, fresh crab, sea urchin, Wagyu skewer ¥800. Arrive early for the freshest produce and the least crowds.
10:00 AM Tsutenkaku Tower ¥1,000 — retro 103m observation tower in Shinsekai, Osaka's symbol since 1912. The neighbourhood around it has been left gloriously unrenovated. Good for a final Osaka photograph and the last kushikatsu meal.
Afternoon Head to Kansai International Airport (KIX) — JR Haruka Express from Namba or Tennoji station, approx 75 min, JR Pass covers it fully. Allow 3 hours before international departure. Terminal 1 for international flights. Duty-free shopping — Japanese whisky, Kit-Kat flavours (the ones you can't get elsewhere), mochi sweets.

Money-Saving Strategies
🏪
Convenience Store Meals

7-Eleven, Lawson, and FamilyMart in Japan are a genuine revelation. Onigiri (rice balls) ¥120, sandwiches ¥200, hot oden soup ¥100/piece, fresh pastries ¥180. Hot foods at the counter. Breakfast from a conbini every 2–3 days saves ₹3,000–4,000 over the trip without sacrificing quality.

🍜
Lunch vs Dinner Pricing

Many good restaurants offer identical food at lunch for 40–50% less than dinner. A ¥2,500 dinner set is often ¥1,200 at lunch. Plan your bigger meals as lunches. Kaiseki (multi-course Japanese cuisine) at dinner is ¥8,000–15,000 per person; the same restaurant's lunch set is ¥2,500–3,500.

🎌
Free Temples & Shrines

Many of Japan's most beautiful sites are free. Fushimi Inari — free. Meiji Shrine — free. Arashiyama bamboo grove — free. Gion district walk — free. Nishiki Market — free. Philosopher's Path — free. Budget the ¥500–¥1,300 entry fees strategically for the truly exceptional paid sites.

🚌
Day Pass Buses (Kyoto)

In Kyoto, the city bus ¥600/day pass covers all bus routes. Many temples in Arashiyama, Nishiyama, and Higashiyama are bus-accessible. The Kyoto City Bus network is comprehensive and has English signage. Saves ¥200–300 per journey if you're making 4+ trips a day.

Flight Options from India
Direct Air India flies Delhi → Tokyo Narita (9.5hrs) and Mumbai → Tokyo. IndiGo has seasonal direct routes Mumbai/Delhi → Tokyo. Direct flights ₹35,000–65,000 return. Book 3–4 months ahead for best prices.
Via Hub More options via Singapore Airlines (Singapore), Qatar Airways (Doha), Cathay Pacific (Hong Kong), or ANA/JAL. One-stop options ₹28,000–50,000 return. Singapore layover adds 1–2hrs each way but can be worth the price difference.
Return Fly home from Kansai International (KIX) — Osaka. Saves you the return Shinkansen to Tokyo. JR Haruka Express to KIX: 75 min, JR Pass covers it. Check if your airline flies out of KIX — most major carriers do.

Japan in 10 Days Practical Tips · Page 8
Things That Make Japan Different

8 things to know
before you land

🛂
1. Japan Visa — Plan Ahead

Indians need a Japan tourist visa — no visa on arrival. Apply at VFS Japan centres in Mumbai, Delhi, Chennai, Kolkata, or Bengaluru. Processing: 5–7 working days. Cost: ₹1,700. Required documents: 3 months bank statement, hotel bookings, itinerary, confirmed return flight, employment letter. Apply 3–4 weeks before travel to be safe.

🚄
2. JR Pass — Buy Before Flying

The 7-day JR Pass (¥50,000 = ₹27,500) covers all Shinkansen bullet trains (except Nozomi/Mizuho), Narita Express, and most JR local trains. Cannot be purchased in Japan — only from authorised overseas agents or IRCTC Japan. On this 10-day itinerary, the pass pays for itself on the Tokyo–Kyoto–Osaka–Hiroshima route alone. Activate on Day 1 at the train station.

🏧
3. Cash Culture & ATMs

Japan is largely cash-based. Many restaurants, temples, small shops, and traditional inns (ryokan) do not accept cards. 7-Eleven ATMs and Japan Post ATMs accept foreign Mastercard/Visa/Maestro reliably. Withdraw ¥30,000–50,000 at a time to minimise fees. Keep ¥5,000–10,000 accessible at all times. Never rely on cards alone.

🌸
4. Cherry Blossom Season

Late March to mid-April is Japan's most spectacular and most crowded season. Under cherry trees everything becomes ethereal. Book accommodation 6–8 months in advance for this period — hotels fill completely. Bloom timing varies year to year by 1–2 weeks; monitor Japan Meteorological Corporation forecasts from February. Worth every ounce of planning.

🏪
5. Convenience Stores Are Extraordinary

Japan's convenience stores (7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart) are unlike anything elsewhere in the world. Hot foods, incredibly fresh onigiri, quality sandwiches, hot noodles, excellent coffee, ATMs, printing services, bill payment, and sometimes alcohol. Onigiri ¥120, hot coffee ¥100. Eating one meal a day from a conbini is not a compromise — it's part of the experience.

👟
6. Shoe Etiquette

You will remove your shoes at temple interiors, traditional ryokan guesthouses, many restaurants, and occasionally at some homes and ryokan common areas. Bring slip-on shoes or shoes without complex lacing. Keep your socks clean and hole-free (genuinely). Some places provide indoor slippers — remove those before stepping on tatami mat rooms.

🔇
7. Train & Public Etiquette

No phone calls on trains — step off or text instead. Keep voices low in public spaces. No eating while walking (eat at the stall, sit on a bench, find a konbini corner — never walking down the street). Give up priority seats for elderly and pregnant. Queue in the marked lines on platforms. These aren't strict rules — they're cultural norms everyone follows instinctively. Follow them and you'll feel the difference.

📱
8. Navigation & Translation Apps

Download Google Maps offline for all four cities. Google Translate with Japanese downloaded — point camera at any menu or sign for instant translation. Hyperdia or Jorudan for train route planning with JR Pass compatibility filter. Tenki.jp for the most accurate Japanese weather forecasts. PayPay QR payment app works at many places if you have a Japanese number.

Ryokan Experience
Worth the Premium

A Japanese ryokan (traditional inn) is a cultural experience: tatami floors, yukata robes, communal onsen bath, multi-course kaiseki dinner. Best in Kyoto or near Hakone (day trip from Tokyo). Budget ¥20,000–40,000/person including dinner + breakfast. Non-negotiable experience once per trip.

What to Pack

Packing list for Japan

Japan's trains have no luggage racks for large suitcases (overhead bins are small). Pack lighter than you think you need. A 20kg suitcase on Japanese trains is a liability — a 7kg cabin bag is freedom.

🎒
Light Luggage
Carry-on only if possible. Suitcase shipment service (takkyubin) available hotel-to-hotel ¥1,500–2,000.
👟
Slip-On Shoes
Removed constantly at temples, ryokans, some restaurants. Comfort + easy removal essential.
💴
Cash — ¥30,000+ on Arrival
Top up at 7-Eleven ATMs. Always have ¥5,000–10,000 accessible. Never rely on cards alone.
🎫
JR Pass + Activation Plan
Bought in India. Activate at Narita/Haneda on Day 1. Keep the paper pass safe — it's irreplaceable.
📱
Suica/Pasmo Card Loaded
Get at airport or major JR station. Load ¥3,000 to start. Works on trains, buses, and convenience stores.
🌡️
Layered Clothing
Weather varies significantly city to city. Tokyo in April: 12–20°C. Light jacket + layers. Spring/autumn ideal.
🧴
Toiletries from India
Western deodorant options limited in Japan. Japanese skincare and sunscreen (SPF 50+) are world-class and cheap — stock up.
📄
Japan Visa + All Documents
Visa, passport, travel insurance, hotel bookings, return ticket, JR Pass exchange order. Keep digital copies too.
☂️
Compact Umbrella
Japan gets sudden rain. Convenience stores sell excellent ¥500 umbrellas too. Light rain gear for hiking days.
🎁
Small Gifts from India
Culturally meaningful at ryokans and with hosts. Small boxes of Indian sweets or dry fruits go a long way. The gesture is always remembered.
🔌
Universal Power Adapter
Japan uses Type A plugs (flat two-pin, 100V). Indian plugs don't fit directly. Bring a compact universal adapter.
💊
Basic Medications from India
Some common Indian medications (even basic OTC) may be controlled substances in Japan. Bring what you need with a doctor's letter if prescription.
